This catalog documents improv games, warmups, exercises, long-form structures, show formats, and class rituals observed in Flying Pig Improv classes, practices, and shows in Wichita, Kansas.

Entries distinguish between:

- **Standard improv practice** — games or exercises commonly found in broader improv/theatre training.
- **Common variation** — recognizable game family with local rule differences.
- **Flying Pig house practice** — instructor-specific or locally specific structure.
- **Unknown / working name** — observed practice where the standard name is unclear.

The catalog prioritizes observed Flying Pig usage over generic improv descriptions.

## Class Structures and Rituals

### Jesse’s Opening Meditation
**Type:** Class Structure / Opening Ritual  
**Standard:** Flying Pig House Practice  
**Variant Type:** Instructor-specific ritual with Eastern / New Age spiritual framing  
**Observed In:** Student Classes  
**Observed Frequency:** Every student class  
**Status:** Core Flying Pig / Jesse classroom ritual; documented for curriculum accuracy

**Purpose**
- Transition students from ordinary life into class readiness.
- Ground students in breath, body, and presence.
- Establish a room where risk-taking is expected.
- Reduce judgment of other players.
- Reduce self-judgment.
- Create a shared emotional and attentional reset before improv work begins.
- Reinforce Jesse’s teaching values before games, exercises, and scene work.

**Known Flying Pig / Jesse Structure**

The meditation begins with breath.

Students are asked to breathe deeply from the gut or belly, rather than from the chest or from “good posture.” Jesse frames this as breathing from the place where chi lives. Students are asked to close their eyes.

Jesse then directs students to notice what they see with their eyes closed: floaters, light, images, figments of imagination, or whatever appears in the darkness.

Students are asked to imagine a grounding cord running down the spine and extending all the way to the center of the earth. They are directed to feel their energy.

The meditation then expands into presence and gratitude. Students are asked to think of all the people and all the choices those people had to make for each student to be right here, right now.

Jesse then leads a body-awareness scan. Students are asked to feel their:
- toes,
- feet,
- ankles,
- legs,
- torso,
- shoulders,
- neck,
- head,
- fingertips.

After breath, grounding, energy, and body awareness, Jesse asks students to make three agreements.

**Agreement 1: Take a risk.**  
Students are asked to do something they have not done before. Jesse frames doing new things as the way people grow.

**Agreement 2: Let go of judgment of others.**  
Students are asked to release judgment of other people in the room. Jesse emphasizes that personal opinion is not useful during play and may use strong comic language to make the point.

**Agreement 3: Let go of judgment of yourself.**  
Jesse frames this as the hardest agreement. Students are asked to quiet the internal voices in the room for the length of class. The point is not therapy or deep personal analysis; the point is to put self-judgment away during class and allow play to happen.

Jesse references *The Four Agreements* as the source or inspiration for the agreement framework and may recommend that students read it.

After the three agreements, Jesse returns students to breath.

Students are asked to take a deep breath and raise their hands high in the air. On a count of three, they let their hands down with an audible sigh.

This breath / raised hands / audible sigh sequence is repeated three times.

After the final breath and sigh, students are asked to open their eyes.

**Approximate Timing**
- The meditation commonly runs 8–18 minutes.
- The core order remains consistent, but Jesse may circle through breathing, grounding, energy, body awareness, and agreement language at length.
- Repetition and meandering appear to be part of the actual delivery style rather than an accidental deviation.

**Terminology / Spiritual Framing**
- Jesse’s meditation uses Eastern / New Age spiritual vocabulary.
- Key terms and concepts include chi, energy, grounding, grounding cord, and sometimes opening awareness or the third eye.
- This terminology appears important to Jesse’s version of the ritual, not merely decorative.
- The meditation is not framed as a neutral breathing exercise; it is part of the specific Flying Pig / Jesse classroom container.

**Skills and Class Behaviors Supported**
- Presence.
- Breath awareness.
- Physical grounding.
- Group focus.
- Risk tolerance.
- Reduced self-monitoring.
- Reduced judgment of scene partners.
- Emotional openness.
- Readiness to commit.
- Shared class cohesion.
- Willingness to fail visibly.
- Transition from ordinary social behavior into improv play.

**Common Failure Modes / Student Friction**
- The meditation can be one of the most difficult parts of class for some students.
- First-time students may be surprised by the length, tone, repetition, or spiritual framing.
- Some students may disengage before the improv work begins.
- Some first-time students have left during the meditation.
- The eyes-closed portion may feel vulnerable or uncomfortable.
- Students from different religious, cultural, or skeptical backgrounds may resist the Eastern / New Age terminology.
- The repetition and circling can reduce trust for students who prefer concise instruction.
- Students may feel trapped between participating and visibly rejecting the ritual.
- The meditation can create resistance before the actual improv exercises begin.
- Jesse appears aware of this risk and often comments afterward with something like “everybody is still here.”

**Coaching Notes**
- This meditation is not a standard improv warmup.
- It is better understood as a class-opening ritual or classroom container.
- The functional sequence appears to be:
  - breath,
  - eyes closed,
  - inward visual awareness,
  - grounding cord,
  - energy awareness,
  - gratitude / presence,
  - body scan,
  - three agreements,
  - return to breath,
  - group exhale,
  - open eyes.
- The three agreements directly support improv behavior:
  - risk supports bold offers,
  - releasing judgment of others supports acceptance,
  - releasing self-judgment supports commitment and recovery.
- The meditation reflects Jesse’s broader teaching priorities: risk, commitment, vulnerability, anti-judgment, and readiness for exposed performance.
- The ritual carries real onboarding risk. For aligned students, it may create safety and shared focus. For skeptical or new students, it may create discomfort, impatience, or distrust before the first exercise begins.
- If adapted by another instructor, the functional purpose should be preserved; however, at Flying Pig, Jesse appears to treat the specific meditation as a house requirement rather than an interchangeable mindfulness exercise.

**Flying Pig Specific Notes**
- Jesse reportedly leads this before every student class.
- It is unclear whether she uses it before troupe practices.
- Jesse has indicated that anyone who wants to teach a class at her theater needs to do the meditation the way she does it.
- Troy once volunteered to lead the meditation and asked whether he could use a different meditation learned while working in a drug rehab center.
- Jesse rejected the substitution and emphasized that the meditation should be done in her established form.
- Troy’s attempted version hit the key components in the wrong order.
- Jesse later praised the effort and gave a fuller rundown of the required structure.
- Troy’s interpretation is that Jesse may have been especially concerned about alternate religious framing because of Troy’s Christian background, although Jesse did not explicitly state that.
- The meditation Troy had intended to offer was not explicitly Christian; it was closer to a mindfulness / perspective-shifting exercise involving zooming far out from the body and returning.
- This episode suggests that the meditation is not incidental. It is part of Flying Pig’s teaching identity and Jesse’s control of the classroom environment.

### Where Have Your Fingers Been
**Type:** Warmup  
**Standard:** Unknown / Flying Pig observed warmup  
**Observed In:** Class Warmups

**Purpose**
- Warm up quick character and scene creation.
- Practice tiny physical performance through the hands.
- Build comfort with playful silliness.
- Encourage fast, simple three-line scenes.
- Train players to make clear offers with limited tools.

**Rules / Flying Pig Structure**
- Players stand in a circle.
- Before each turn, the group chants:
  - “Where have your fingers been, oh where have your fingers been, whoo, whoo.”
- One player gives the next player a setting or prompt.
- Prompt examples include:
  - in the kitchen,
  - at Starbucks,
  - on an airplane.
- The receiving player uses their index fingers as characters or puppets.
- The player acts out a short three-line finger scene inspired by the prompt.
- After performing, that player gives a new setting or prompt to the next player.
- The pattern continues around the circle.

**Skills Developed**
- Fast initiation.
- Character simplification.
- Physical specificity.
- Playful commitment.
- Three-line scene structure.
- Making limited physical tools expressive.
- Accepting a prompt quickly.
- Passing useful prompts to the next player.

**Common Failure Modes**
- Finger puppeteering is unclear or too small.
- The player hesitates too long before beginning.
- The three-line scene becomes vague or does not clearly use the prompt.
- The performer talks as themselves instead of letting the fingers play the scene.
- The scene drags beyond the quick three-line structure.
- The next prompt is too vague, private, or hard to play quickly.
- Players become self-conscious and reduce commitment.

**Coaching Notes**
- The scene should be short and clear.
- The fingers should behave like playable characters, not just point vaguely.
- Speed matters more than polish.
- A simple relationship or conflict is enough.
- The performer should commit to the tiny puppet logic rather than apologizing for it.
- Jesse mainly reinforces the rules and structure rather than heavily coaching the scene content.

**Flying Pig Specific Notes**
- Flying Pig runs this as a circle game.
- The group chant is documented as: “Where have your fingers been, oh where have your fingers been, whoo, whoo.”
- A player gives a setting or prompt to the next player.
- The performer acts out a three-line scene using index fingers.
- The performer then gives the next prompt.
- Common struggles are finger puppeteering, clarity, and speed.

**Post-V4 Reconciliation Additions**
- Circle game.
- Group chant precedes each turn.
- Prompt examples:
  - In the kitchen
  - At Starbucks
  - On an airplane
- Player performs a 3-line finger scene.
- Jesse mainly reinforces rules.

### Tongue Twisters
**Type:** Warmup / Articulation Exercise  
**Standard:** Yes  
**Observed In:** Class Warmups

**Purpose**
- Warm up articulation.
- Improve stage speech clarity.
- Practice clean vowels and consonants.
- Train players to finish words instead of swallowing final sounds.
- Prepare the voice and mouth for louder, clearer performance.

**Rules / Flying Pig Structure**
- Tongue Twisters are not a formal programmed exercise with a fixed Flying Pig structure.
- Jesse or a guest director provides a phrase.
- Phrases rotate rather than following a single permanent list.
- The group repeats or works the phrase as directed.
- The emphasis is clarity over speed.
- Players focus on:
  - clear vowels,
  - full consonants,
  - final sounds,
  - and clean articulation.

**Skills Developed**
- Diction.
- Vocal clarity.
- Mouth agility.
- Projection readiness.
- Awareness of swallowed sounds.
- Precision under repetition.

**Common Failure Modes**
- Rushing for speed before the phrase is clear.
- Dropping final consonants.
- Mumbling through difficult sounds.
- Flattening vowels.
- Treating the exercise as a race instead of articulation practice.
- Letting the phrase become quieter as it gets harder.

**Coaching Notes**
- Clarity is more important than speed.
- Final sounds matter.
- Vowels should stay open and intentional.
- The goal is stage-readiness, not merely getting through the tongue twister.
- This can remain a shorter catalog entry because Flying Pig does not appear to treat it as a formal game with a fixed sequence.

**Flying Pig Specific Notes**
- Jesse or a guest director provides the phrase.
- There is no fixed Flying Pig format currently documented.
- Phrases rotate.
- The focus is articulation: vowels, consonants, and final sounds.

**Post-V4 Reconciliation Additions**
- No fixed Flying Pig format.
- Director provides a phrase.
- Focus:
  - Clear vowels
  - Full consonants
  - Articulation
- Clarity is more important than speed.

### A to B
**Type:** Warmup  
**Standard:** Yes  
**Common Names:** A to B; Word Association; Word Association Circle  
**Observed In:** Class Warmups

**Purpose**
- Warm up associative thinking.
- Reduce hesitation.
- Practice accepting the first available idea.
- Build focus and listening in the group.
- Encourage players to respond without over-editing themselves.

**Rules**
- Players stand in a circle.
- Player 1 says a random word.
- Player 2 immediately says the first word that comes into their head.
- The next player responds to Player 2’s word with the first word that comes into their head.
- The association continues around the circle.
- The goal is speed, honesty, and flow rather than cleverness.
- If the circle is large enough, multiple words may be started and passed around the circle at the same time.

**Common Variants**
- **A to C:** Instead of saying the first associated word, the player mentally notices the first association, discards it, and says the second association.
- **A to B, No Nouns:** Players continue normal word association, but may not use nouns.
- **Multiple Words:** In a larger circle, several starting words are active at once, requiring players to track incoming offers and respond quickly.

**Skills Developed**
- Free association.
- Listening.
- Fast response.
- Letting go of cleverness.
- Pattern disruption.
- Mental flexibility.
- Group rhythm.
- Comfort with imperfect offers.
- Reduced self-censorship.

**Common Failure Modes**
- Players try to be funny instead of honest.
- Players pause too long searching for a clever association.
- Players repeat obvious associations too safely.
- In A to C, players accidentally give the first association instead of the second.
- In No Nouns, players default back into objects, names, or places.
- With multiple words, players lose track of which word is coming to them.
- The circle becomes too polite or slow, reducing the warmup value.

**Coaching Notes**
- The first goal is momentum.
- In standard A to B, the “obvious” association is usually correct.
- In A to C, the value comes from stepping past the most obvious response into a less predictable but still connected idea.
- Players should avoid judging whether the association is good.
- Mistakes should be accepted and moved through quickly.
- The exercise is not about vocabulary; it is about lowering the filter.
- Multiple-word versions increase load and force players to stay present.

**Flying Pig Specific Notes**
- Flying Pig uses A to B as a circle warmup.
- Flying Pig may start multiple words when the circle is large enough.
- Flying Pig uses A to C as a variant where players discard the first association and give the second.
- Flying Pig also uses “A to B, No Nouns” as a constraint variant.
- The exercise aligns with Flying Pig’s broader teaching emphasis on fast offers, acceptance, recovery, and not overthinking.

**Post-V4 Reconciliation Additions**
- Circle game.
- One player says a word.
- Next player associates from the immediately previous word.
- Usually starts as A-to-B.
- Often graduates to A-to-C.
- Variant:
  - No nouns.
- Goal:
  - Speed
  - Immediate response
  - Listening

### Bunny Bunny
**Type:** Warmup  
**Standard:** Yes  
**Common Names:** Bunny Bunny; Bunny Bunny Ticky Tocky  
**Observed In:** Class Warmups

**Purpose**
- Build group rhythm.
- Warm up focus and reaction speed.
- Practice passing attention clearly.
- Encourage vocal and physical commitment.
- Build comfort with playful silliness.
- Increase group energy through escalating tempo and controlled chaos.

**Rules**
- Players stand in a circle.
- The group establishes a rhythmic chant, usually “Kum-chak-a, Kum-chak-a.”
- One player starts with the bunny by holding up two fingers on each hand, facing toward themselves.
- While facing the fingers toward themselves, the player says “Bunny bunny.”
- The player then turns the two-finger bunny gesture toward another player and says “Bunny bunny,” passing the bunny to that player.
- The receiving player repeats the pattern:
  - bunny toward self: “Bunny bunny,”
  - bunny toward another player: “Bunny bunny.”
- The rest of the circle continues chanting the rhythm underneath the exchange.
- After the basic version is established, an additional rule is added.
- The players immediately to either side of the active bunny raise their hands and say “Ticky tocky, ticky tocky.”
- The game speeds up over time and becomes increasingly chaotic.

**Skills Developed**
- Rhythm.
- Focus.
- Eye contact.
- Fast reaction.
- Group listening.
- Physical commitment.
- Vocal commitment.
- Pattern tracking.
- Recovery from mistakes.
- Comfort with high-energy ensemble play.

**Common Failure Modes**
- The rhythm falls apart.
- Players pass the bunny without clear eye contact or targeting.
- The active bunny hesitates too long.
- Side players miss their “Ticky tocky” cue.
- Players become self-conscious and reduce the physical or vocal commitment.
- The chant becomes too quiet to support the group rhythm.
- Speed increases before the group has established control.
- Players laugh or collapse after mistakes instead of staying in the pattern.

**Coaching Notes**
- The game works best when the chant is strong and shared.
- The bunny pass should be clear, physical, and directed.
- The side-player “Ticky tocky” role keeps everyone engaged, not just the active bunny.
- Mistakes are part of the warmup; recover and keep the rhythm moving.
- Speed should increase gradually.
- The goal is not perfection; the goal is collective focus under playful pressure.
- Players should commit vocally and physically even when the game becomes silly.
- The exercise is useful because it combines rhythm, attention, eye contact, and ensemble energy.

**Flying Pig Specific Notes**
- Flying Pig uses the chant “Kum-chak-a, Kum-chak-a” underneath the bunny passing.
- The active player uses two fingers on each hand as the bunny.
- The bunny is first turned toward the active player while saying “Bunny bunny,” then turned toward another player while saying “Bunny bunny.”
- Flying Pig adds the “Ticky tocky, ticky tocky” rule for the players immediately to either side of the active bunny.
- The warmup speeds up over time and becomes somewhat chaotic.
- Troy likes this warmup and considers it effective.
- The exercise fits Flying Pig’s preference for high-energy group focus, fast recovery, and playful commitment.

**Post-V4 Reconciliation Additions**
- Circle game.
- Side players perform Toki Toki.
- Remaining players perform Kimcha-ka Um Chaka.
- Speed and volume increase over time.
- Goal is energy and focus more than coaching.


### Bibbity Bibbity Bop
**Type:** Warmup / Focus Exercise / Reaction Circle Game  
**Standard:** Yes / Common improv warmup with Flying Pig rule set  
**Common Names:** Bibbity Bibbity Bop  
**Observed In:** Class Warmups

**Purpose**
- Build focus and reaction speed.
- Train players to listen precisely under pressure.
- Practice rule tracking as new calls are added.
- Encourage fast physical commitment.
- Develop side-player awareness.
- Practice recovery from mistakes without embarrassment.
- Warm up group energy through playful pressure.

**Setup**
- Players stand in a circle.
- One player begins in the center of the circle.
- The center player turns to a player in the circle and initiates a task by saying one of the active calls.
- If the targeted player or either required side player fails the task, the failing player swaps into the center.
- The former center player takes that player’s place in the circle.
- Rules are added over time rather than all at once.

**Core Rule / Swap Mechanic**
- The player in the center initiates a call.
- The targeted player and sometimes the adjacent players must respond correctly.
- If a required player fails, that player goes to the center.
- Failure may include saying the wrong thing, speaking when they should not speak, failing to move, moving incorrectly, missing the seven-second window, or failing to coordinate with the adjacent players.
- If multiple players fail, the coach or group may decide who swaps in, usually based on the clearest or first failure.

**Flying Pig Rule Set**

**1. Bibbity Bibbity Bop**
- The center player points at a player and says: “Bibbity Bibbity Bop.”
- The targeted player must say “Bop” before the center player finishes saying their own final “Bop.”
- If the targeted player does not say “Bop” in time, they fail and swap into the center.

**2. Bop**
- The center player points at a player and says: “Bop.”
- The targeted player must say nothing.
- If the targeted player speaks, they fail and swap into the center.

**3. Washing Machine**
- The center player points at a player and says: “Washing Machine.”
- The targeted player has seven seconds to make washing-machine sounds while moving their hands back and forth.
- The players immediately to the left and right of the targeted player must say “Chugga Chugga” while moving their hands toward the player doing the washing machine.
- If the targeted player or either adjacent player fails, the failing player swaps into the center.

**4. Elephant**
- The center player points at a player and says: “Elephant.”
- The targeted player has seven seconds to make an elephant trunk with their arms and make an elephant trumpet sound.
- The players immediately to the left and right of the targeted player use their hands to form the elephant’s ears.
- If the targeted player or either adjacent player fails, the failing player swaps into the center.

**5. Red Baron**
- The center player points at a player and says: “Red Baron.”
- The targeted player has seven seconds to make goggles with their hands and scream as if they are in a crashing plane.
- The players immediately to the left and right of the targeted player stretch their arms out straight to form wings.

### Zoom, Schwartz, Mafigliano
**Type:** Warmup / Focus Exercise / Passing Circle Game  
**Standard:** Common improv warmup family with Flying Pig local call words and added rules  
**Common Names:** Zoom, Schwartz, Mafigliano; standard family often known as Zoom / Schwartz / Profigliano or Perfigliano  
**Observed In:** Class Warmups

**Purpose**
- Build focus and reaction speed.
- Practice passing attention clearly.
- Train players to track direction, targets, blocks, and rule exceptions.
- Develop fast recovery after mistakes.
- Increase cognitive flexibility as new rules are layered in.
- Encourage eye contact, decisive pointing, and whole-circle awareness.

**Setup**
- Players stand in a circle.
- One player is the active player.
- The active player uses one of the available calls to pass active-player status to someone else.
- Rules are added over time.
- The game becomes increasingly complex as players must track adjacent players, non-adjacent players, blocks, skips, behind-the-back targets, and forced chains.

**Core Principle**
- The game is about passing the active player.
- A player must know how they became active, because some calls are restricted based on the incoming pass.
- The call word, gaze, pointing, and direction determine who becomes active next.

**Flying Pig Rule Set**

**1. Zoom**
- The active player says “Zoom” and points to a player who is not adjacent.
- The targeted non-adjacent player becomes active.
- A player may not use Zoom if they became active through a Zoom.
- In other words, a player cannot respond to a Zoom with another Zoom.

**2. Mafigliano**
- The active player says “Mafigliano” and passes active-player status to the player immediately to their right or left.
- The direction of the pass is indicated by the player’s focus, gesture, or body orientation.
- This is the basic adjacent-player pass.

**3. Schwartz**
- If active-player status is being passed to a player, that player may cross their arms and say “Schwartz.”
- Schwartz blocks the pass.
- The player who attempted the pass must choose someone else or otherwise redirect according to the active rule.
- Schwartz does not make the blocking player active; it rejects the incoming pass.

**4. Bork**
- The active player says “Bork” and looks either to the player on their left or right.
- The pass does not go to the player being looked at.
- Instead, it goes to the next player in the circle past that adjacent player.
- Bork is therefore a one-player skip in the indicated direction.

**5. Twizzler**
- The active player says “Twizzler” and looks either to the player on their left or right.
- The pass does not go to the adjacent player being looked at.
- Instead, it goes to the player behind the active player.
- This requires the circle to understand “behind” relative to the active player.

**6. Twizzler Bork**
- The active player says “Twizzler Bork” and looks either to the player on their left or right.
- The pass does not go to the adjacent player being looked at.
- It also does not go merely to the player behind the active player.
- Instead, it goes to the player on the far side of the player behind the active player.
- This combines the behind-the-player logic of Twizzler with the extra skip logic of Bork.

**7. ZoomaZoomaZooma**
- The active player says “ZoomaZoomaZooma” and passes active-player status to someone who is not adjacent.
- The receiving player is locked into doing ZoomaZoomaZooma to another non-adjacent player.
- That next receiving player is also locked into doing ZoomaZoomaZooma.
- ZoomaZoomaZooma always goes in chains of three.
- If a ZoomaZoomaZooma is blocked with Schwartz, the player who attempted it still owes a ZoomaZoomaZooma and must pick another target.
- The forced chain continues until the required three ZoomaZoomaZooma passes have completed.

**Skills Developed**
- Focus.
- Eye contact.
- Fast decision-making.
- Direction tracking.
- Rule memory.
- Blocking and redirection.
- Peripheral awareness.
- Whole-circle attention.
- Recovery under pressure.
- Cognitive flexibility.
- Group rhythm.

**Common Failure Modes**
- Zooming after receiving a Zoom.
- Passing to an adjacent player with Zoom.
- Forgetting that Schwartz blocks the pass rather than becoming a new pass.
- Failing to redirect after being Schwartzed.
- Looking at the wrong player for Bork, Twizzler, or Twizzler Bork.
- Passing to the player being looked at instead of the rule-defined target.
- Losing track of who is behind the active player.
- Forgetting that ZoomaZoomaZooma must happen in a chain of three.
- Treating a Schwartzed ZoomaZoomaZooma as canceled instead of still owed.
- Hesitating too long while calculating the target.
- Players disengage when they are not near the active player and then miss a sudden Zoom or ZoomaZoomaZooma.

**Coaching Notes**
- Add the rules gradually.
- Start with Zoom and Mafigliano until the circle understands non-adjacent and adjacent passing.
- Add Schwartz to introduce blocking and redirection.
- Add Bork, Twizzler, and Twizzler Bork only after the basic pass/block mechanics are stable.
- Add ZoomaZoomaZooma last because it requires chain tracking and rule memory.
- Players should make clear eye contact, point or gesture decisively, and speak loudly.
- The goal is speed and recovery, not legalistic perfection.
- When a mistake happens, correct it quickly and keep the game moving.
- The game works best when the group treats confusion as part of the fun rather than a reason to stop.

**Flying Pig Specific Notes**
- Flying Pig uses the call **Mafigliano**, not Profigliano, Perfigliano, or Magliano.
- Flying Pig uses the spelling **Schwartz**.
- The documented Flying Pig calls are:
  - Zoom,
  - Mafigliano,
  - Schwartz,
  - Bork,
  - Twizzler,
  - Twizzler Bork,
  - ZoomaZoomaZooma.
- Bork, Twizzler, Twizzler Bork, and ZoomaZoomaZooma are added later in the progression.
- ZoomaZoomaZooma is always a three-part forced chain.
- A Schwartzed ZoomaZoomaZooma does not cancel the obligation; the player must choose another target.

**What Success Looks Like**
- Passes are clear and fast.
- The circle tracks the active player without drifting.
- Blocks are handled cleanly.
- Players recover from errors without shame or long explanation.
- Later rules increase focus and energy rather than collapsing the game.
- The group stays engaged because anyone may become active at any moment.

### Mind Meld
**Type:** Exercise  
**Standard:** Yes  
**Common Names:** Mind Meld; Jinx; Convergence  
**Observed In:** Class Exercises

**Purpose**
- Practice associative thinking.
- Build shared mental pathways between players.
- Train players to listen to and connect offers.
- Encourage active commitment to a guess.
- Develop ensemble intuition and group convergence.
- Reduce overthinking by rewarding quick, confident associations.

**Rules**
- Two players lock eyes.
- Together, they pump one hand in a karate-chop motion while counting down: “3, 2, 1.”
- On the count, each player says a random word at the same time.
- Two new players then lock eyes, repeat the countdown, and try to say the same word.
- Their goal is to connect the two words spoken by the previous pair.
- The exercise continues with new pairs attempting to converge on the same word.
- The sequence continues until two players successfully say the same word at the same time.

**Example**
- Pair 1 says: “dog” and “house.”
- Pair 2 might both try to converge on: “pet,” “yard,” “home,” or “kennel.”
- If they match, the group succeeds.
- If they do not match, the next pair uses those two new words as the basis for the next attempt.

**Skills Developed**
- Association.
- Eye contact.
- Shared focus.
- Listening.
- Pattern recognition.
- Offer connection.
- Fast decision-making.
- Ensemble intuition.
- Commitment to a choice.
- Recovery after mismatch.

**Common Failure Modes**
- Passive players wait for the “obvious” answer instead of committing.
- Players overthink and choose clever associations instead of strong connective ones.
- Players ignore one of the two prior words.
- Players say a word loosely related to their own thought process but not to the shared prompt.
- Players hesitate or soften their vocal commitment.
- The group becomes frustrated if convergence takes too long.
- Players try to explain their reasoning after each miss, slowing the exercise.
- The exercise stalls when players do not actively seek the midpoint between the two words.

**Coaching Notes**
- The goal is convergence, not originality.
- Strong choices usually connect both previous words, not just one.
- Obvious is good.
- Players should commit vocally and physically to the word they choose.
- Eye contact matters because the exercise is partly about shared timing and mutual impulse.
- Misses are useful because they create the next pair of words.
- Players should not debate the logic of a miss; move immediately to the next attempt.
- The exercise works best when players are active, decisive, and willing to be simple.
- Passive players weaken the exercise because convergence requires choosing, not waiting.

**Related Exercises**
- Jesse once described Mind Meld as similar to an exercise she sometimes does called “Object Object Concept.”
- This exercise has not yet been observed by Troy.
- Because its rules are unknown and the name is not confirmed as a standard improv exercise, it should not be formally cataloged until observed.

**Flying Pig Specific Notes**
- Jesse has referred to this exercise as Mind Meld.
- Troy initially knew or described it as Jinx, but Mind Meld should be treated as the primary catalog name.
- Flying Pig uses this as an exercise.
- Troy likes this exercise and considers it one of his favorites.
- The exercise does not work well with passive players.
- Flying Pig’s version uses a karate-chop hand pump with the spoken countdown “3, 2, 1.”
- Each new pair tries to connect the two words used by the previous pair.
- The exercise continues until two players successfully say the same word.
- The exercise aligns with Flying Pig’s broader emphasis on eye contact, fast offers, acceptance, and shared ensemble focus.

### Big Booty
**Type:** Warmup / Rhythm and Focus Game  
**Standard:** Standard improv/theatre warmup; Flying Pig observed  
**Observed In:** Flying Pig class warmups  
**Status:** Reviewed / Troy-confirmed

**Purpose**
- Build group rhythm.
- Warm up focus and listening.
- Practice vocal commitment.
- Train quick recognition and response under beat pressure.
- Practice recovery after mistakes.
- Build comfort with playful failure.
- Energize the room before heavier scene work.

**Setup**
- Players stand in a circle, horseshoe, or line.
- One player is designated Big Booty.
- The remaining players are numbered in order: Number 1, Number 2, Number 3, and so on.
- The group establishes a shared rhythm using thigh slaps and chanting.
- Flying Pig's observed version uses thigh slaps and chanting as the rhythm base.

**Sequence of Play**
1. The group begins the chant/rhythm.
2. Big Booty starts the call sequence.
3. The active player calls their own role or number, then calls another player's role or number.
4. Example structure:
   - "Big Booty, Number Three."
   - Number Three responds: "Number Three, Number Seven."
   - Number Seven responds: "Number Seven, Big Booty."
5. The called player must respond on rhythm.
6. The rhythm continues underneath the calls.
7. If a player misses the rhythm, says the wrong number, hesitates too long, fails to respond, or otherwise breaks the pattern, they move to the end/back or reset position depending on the local version.
8. Numbers shift after a mistake.
9. The game restarts and usually increases in speed or energy as the group stabilizes.

**Known Chant / Jingle**
- The exact Flying Pig wording has not been fully documented.
- The known structure includes thigh slaps and chanting.
- Standard versions often include a chant built around: "Big Booty," "Ah yeah," and numbered player calls.
- Preserve the exact Flying Pig chant if later recovered.

**Important Rules**
- The active player must identify themselves first, then pass to another player.
- Players must stay on the beat.
- Players must track their current number, especially after someone misses and numbers shift.
- The group rhythm matters as much as the verbal call.
- Mistakes should be handled quickly and playfully.
- The game should not stop for long explanations after each miss.

**Skills Developed**
- Rhythm.
- Focus.
- Listening.
- Vocal commitment.
- Number tracking.
- Fast response.
- Group energy.
- Recovery from mistakes.
- Comfort with playful pressure.
- Ensemble attention.

**Common Failure Modes**
- Players lose the beat.
- Players forget their current number after a reset.
- Players respond with the wrong number.
- Players call a number that no longer exists.
- Players hesitate while trying to remember who is who.
- The chant becomes too quiet to support the group.
- Players become embarrassed after mistakes and collapse the energy.
- The game gets too fast before beginners understand the pattern.
- Players focus only on their own number and stop listening to the whole group.

**Coaching Notes**
- Keep the thigh-slap rhythm strong.
- The chant should support the players, not bury them.
- Start slowly enough that the group can succeed.
- Increase speed only after the structure is stable.
- Mistakes are part of the game; reset quickly and keep the energy up.
- Vocal commitment helps the game feel playful instead of tentative.
- The point is not perfection. The point is shared rhythm, attention, and recovery.
- With new players, confirm that everyone understands their current number after each reset.
- Strong versions feel musical, focused, silly, and fast.

**What Success Looks Like**
- The group maintains a clear rhythm.
- Players respond quickly and loudly.
- Number changes are tracked cleanly after mistakes.
- Misses produce laughter and reset energy rather than embarrassment.
- The game speeds up without becoming chaotic too early.
- The room feels more focused, vocal, and energized afterward.

**Flying Pig Specific Notes**
- Troy confirmed Flying Pig has done this game.
- Flying Pig's version uses thigh slaps and chanting.
- Troy recognized the standard Big Booty structure as exactly the game Flying Pig did.
- The exact Flying Pig chant/jingle wording is not fully documented yet and should be preserved if later recovered.
- Catalog this as a standard warmup with Flying Pig-observed rhythm details.

### Mirroring
**Type:** Exercise  
**Standard:** Yes  
**Observed In:** Class Exercises / Pair Work

**Purpose**
- Physical listening.
- Awareness.
- Connection.
- Shared attention.
- Nonverbal agreement.
- Precise physical observation.
- Reducing the impulse to control the scene.

**Rules / Flying Pig Structure**
- Players work in pairs.
- Partners stand facing each other.
- The exercise is leaderless from the start.
- Both players begin moving slowly enough that the other person can follow.
- Leadership should shift naturally every few seconds.
- The goal is to create the illusion that neither player is leading.
- Players mirror the full body, not just the hands.
- Players should copy:
  - face,
  - hands,
  - arms,
  - shoulders,
  - torso,
  - posture,
  - foot position,
  - weight shifts,
  - tempo,
  - and overall movement quality.
- There is no speaking.

**Skills Developed**
- Physical listening.
- Observation.
- Shared control.
- Patience.
- Eye contact.
- Partner awareness.
- Full-body copying.
- Sensitivity to tempo and weight.
- Letting leadership pass back and forth without negotiation.

**Common Failure Modes**
- One player obviously leads for too long.
- The follower waits passively instead of contributing to shared motion.
- Leadership changes are too abrupt.
- Players only copy hands and arms while ignoring the rest of the body.
- Players move too quickly for the partner to mirror accurately.
- Players make movement choices too small to read.
- Players become self-conscious and stop committing physically.
- The pair turns the exercise into a guessing game rather than shared movement.

**Coaching Notes**
- The illusion of no leader matters more than clever movement.
- Move slowly enough that your partner can actually copy you.
- Leadership should pass back and forth naturally, not through an announced handoff.
- Jesse commonly corrects over-leading.
- Jesse also corrects incomplete copying, especially when players mirror only the hands instead of the entire body.
- The exercise is successful when an outside observer cannot easily tell who is leading.
- This is not about dance skill. It is about attention, softness, and precise physical agreement.

**Flying Pig Specific Notes**
- Flying Pig runs this in standing pairs facing each other.
- The exercise is treated as leaderless from the beginning.
- Leadership should shift every few seconds.
- The goal is exact copying and the illusion of shared impulse.
- The most common coaching points are:
  - switch leaders more often,
  - stop over-leading,
  - slow down,
  - and mirror the whole body rather than only the hands.

**Post-V4 Reconciliation Additions**

### I Am a Tree
**Type:** Exercise  
**Standard:** Yes  
**Observed In:** Class Exercises / Circle Work

**Purpose**
- Yes-And.
- Association.
- Embodiment.
- Physical offers.
- Fast scenic connection.
- Building from the immediately previous gift.
- Practicing simple group composition.

**Rules / Flying Pig Structure**
- Players stand in a circle.
- One player enters the center and says, “I am a tree.”
- That player physically embodies a tree.
- Player 2 enters and modifies Player 1’s offer.
- Player 3 enters and modifies Player 2’s offer.
- After the three-person image is established, Player 1 chooses one of the other two players to send away.
- The remaining player stays in the center and restates their object or concept.
- The remaining player’s offer becomes the new starting point.
- The exercise continues in the same pattern.
- The game may continue until someone eventually returns to “I am a tree.”

**Example Structure**
- Player 1: “I am a tree.”
- Player 2: “I am an apple hanging from the tree.”
- Player 3: “I am a worm inside the apple.”
- Player 1 sends away one player.
- If the worm remains, the worm restates: “I am a worm.”
- New players enter and modify the worm.

**Important Flying Pig Rule**
- Player 2 modifies Player 1.
- Player 3 modifies Player 2.
- Players should build from the immediately previous object or concept, not jump back to an earlier object unless that is the actual current offer.

**Skills Developed**
- Yes-And.
- Association.
- Physical commitment.
- Object and concept embodiment.
- Listening to the immediate prior offer.
- Group composition.
- Letting simple offers evolve.
- Entering the center decisively.
- Avoiding passive watching.

**Common Failure Modes**
- Player 3 modifies Player 1 instead of Player 2.
- Players stay too concrete when a more interesting association is available.
- Players make weak physical choices.
- Players describe an object without embodying it.
- Players stay outside the circle too long instead of joining.
- Players try to be clever rather than clearly connected.
- The remaining player forgets to restate their object.
- Players do not understand which offer is now the active anchor.

**Coaching Notes**
- Modify the immediately previous object.
- Join the game rather than staying safely outside the circle.
- Embody the offer physically.
- Abstract concepts are allowed and often praised when they are clear and playable.
- The exercise works best when players accept the current object as the new center of the world.
- A strong offer is connected, physically readable, and easy for the next player to build from.

**Flying Pig Specific Notes**
- Flying Pig starts specifically with “I am a tree.”
- Jesse corrects players who modify the wrong prior object.
- Jesse also corrects non-participation when players stay outside the circle instead of entering.
- The exercise may continue until someone eventually returns to “I am a tree.”
- Abstract concepts are often praised if the association is clear and embodied.

**Post-V4 Reconciliation Additions**
- Circle game.
- Structure:
  - Player 1: "I am a tree."
  - Player 2 modifies Player 1.
  - Player 3 modifies Player 2.
- Player 1 chooses one remaining player.
- One player stays and restates their object.
- Exercise continues.
- Common coaching:
  - Modify the immediately previous object.
  - Join the game rather than staying outside the circle.

- The story continues around the circle until it reaches a natural ending.
- When the group hits a natural ending, Jesse calls: “The End.”

**Important Rule**
- Each player contributes exactly one word.

- Listening.
- Acceptance.
- Sentence construction.
- Group rhythm.
- Grammar awareness.
- Shared narrative control.
- Quick decisions.
- Supporting the immediately previous word.
- Releasing control of plot direction.
- Recognizing when a story has naturally ended.

**Common Failure Modes**
- Hesitation.
- Trying to control the story.
- Skipping a necessary connector word.
- Saying a word that does not make grammatical sense after the previous words.
- Trying to be funny instead of supporting the sentence.
- Ignoring the immediately previous word.
- Planning ahead and then forcing a word that no longer fits.
- Ending too abruptly before the sentence or story is ready.
- Continuing after the story has naturally ended.

- A simple word that preserves grammar is usually better than a clever word that breaks the sentence.
- Players should listen to the last few words, not the story they hoped was happening.
- Jesse commonly helps by repeating the previous few words and encouraging the player to say a word that makes sense.
- The most common failure condition is skipping a connector word that the sentence needs.
- Good players support the sentence in front of them rather than trying to steer the whole story.
- The group should be sensitive to natural endings so the story does not drag after completion.

**Flying Pig Specific Notes**
- Flying Pig runs this as a circle game.
- Jesse usually prompts the group with a starter, commonly “Once upon a time…”
- Each player says exactly one word, with no exceptions.
- The group lands the natural ending.
- Jesse calls “The End” when the ending has been reached.
- Jesse’s correction often involves repeating the previous few words and asking for a word that makes sense.
- The frequent failure point is not wild plot invention; it is often grammar, especially skipping a connector word.

**What Success Looks Like**
- The story moves quickly.
- Each word makes grammatical sense.
- Players support the sentence rather than fighting for control.
- Connector words appear when needed.
- The story reaches a clear natural ending.
- Jesse can call “The End” cleanly because the group has landed the story together.

### Neverending Story
**Type:** Structured Storytelling Exercise  
**Standard:** Common story-spine variation  
**Observed In:** Class Exercises / Circle Storytelling  

**Purpose**
- Narrative causality.
- Listening.
- Story structure.
- Building one beat from the immediately previous beat.
- Practicing consequence-driven storytelling.
- Avoiding random story jumps.
- Learning when a story needs escalation, consequence, and resolution.

**Setup**
- Players stand in a circle.
- Jesse leads the structure by supplying story prompts.
- Players complete each prompt with one sentence.

**Flying Pig Prompt Sequence**
Jesse uses the following prompts:

- “Once upon a time…”
- “One day…”
- “Because of that…”
- “Because of that…”
- “Because of that…”
- “And ever since then…”
- “Moral of the story…”

The “Because of that…” prompt often repeats many times in the middle.

**Rules / Flying Pig Structure**
- Jesse gives a prompt.
- The next player completes that prompt with one sentence.
- Jesse gives the next prompt.
- The next player completes that prompt with one sentence.
- The story continues around the circle.
- “Because of that…” may repeat for multiple players.
- Jesse decides when to stop repeating “Because of that…” and move toward the ending prompts.
- The ending usually moves through:
  - “And ever since then…”
  - “Moral of the story…”

**Example**
- Jesse: “Because of that…”
- Player 1: “The other students all laughed.”
- Jesse: “Because of that…”
- Player 2: “Toby the pumpkin blushed bright red.”
- Jesse: “Because of that…”
- Player 3: “The teacher mistook Toby for a giant tomato.”

**Important Rule**
- Each player gives one sentence.
- The sentence should complete Jesse’s prompt.
- The sentence should respond to the immediately previous story beat, not an earlier beat that the player personally found more interesting.

**Skills Developed**
- Narrative causality.
- Listening to the immediately previous beat.
- Consequence-based storytelling.
- Story structure.
- Restraint.
- Clear sentence-level offers.
- Avoiding contradiction.
- Letting the coach/director shape the story’s ending.
- Recognizing the difference between continuation and random addition.

**Common Failure Modes**
- Breaking causality.
- Responding to an earlier story beat instead of the immediately previous one.
- Modifying a beat that was not directly previous to the player’s turn.
- Contradicting something already established.
- Making a random leap that does not follow from the prior sentence.
- Trying to be funny instead of causal.
- Adding a new unrelated premise instead of continuing the story.
- Dragging the story sideways instead of forward.
- Treating “Because of that…” as “and then something random happened.”

**Coaching Notes**
- “Because of that…” means the next sentence should be a consequence.
- The player should ask: “What happened because of the sentence immediately before mine?”
- The story does not need to be clever; it needs cause and effect.
- Each sentence should preserve what came before.
- Contradictions weaken the shared story.
- A surprising beat is useful only if it still follows from the previous beat.
- Jesse controls when the story moves from middle development into ending.
- Players should not rush to the moral before Jesse gives the ending prompt.
- Strong reps feel like one story being built by many players, not a pile of unrelated funny sentences.

**Flying Pig Specific Notes**
- Flying Pig runs this as a circle game.
- Jesse supplies the prompts.
- Players complete each prompt with one sentence.
- “Because of that…” often repeats many times.
- Jesse decides when to move into “And ever since then…” and “Moral of the story…”
- Jesse’s most common corrections are around causality:
  - breaking causality,
  - modifying something other than the immediately previous beat,
  - and contradicting established information.

**What Success Looks Like**
- Each sentence follows clearly from the previous sentence.
- The story develops through cause and effect.
- Players do not contradict earlier information.
- The repeated “Because of that…” beats build momentum rather than randomness.
- The final “ever since then” and moral feel earned by the story.

- Grammar matters.

- A simple complete cowboy-style saying is better than a long, tortured sentence.

**Flying Pig Specific Notes**

- There is no fixed Jesse starter phrase currently documented.
- The group yells “YEE HAW” when the sentence reaches a natural ending.

- The exercise is closely related to D’Jever in that both reward clean sentence completion and restraint.

**Flying Pig Specific Notes**

### 5 Second Delay
**Type:** Exercise  
**Standard:** Common Constraint Exercise / Exact Name Unknown  
**Observed In:** Student Classes  
**Observed Frequency:** Rare

**Purpose**
- Slow players down.
- Increase listening.
- Reduce reflexive chatter.
- Create space for emotional reaction before dialogue.
- Train players to let silence exist onstage.
- Encourage physical behavior, eye contact, and internal processing between lines.
- Help players discover that scenes do not need constant talking to stay alive.

**Rules**
- Two players perform a scene.
- Players must leave at least five seconds between lines of dialogue.
- During the silence, players should remain active through:
  - eye contact,
  - physical behavior,
  - emotional reaction,
  - object work,
  - status shifts,
  - listening,
  - letting the previous line affect them.
- The scene continues normally, but the pace of spoken dialogue is deliberately slowed.
- The delay applies between lines, not merely between speeches.

**Observed Runtime**
- One observed class of roughly 14 students took about an hour to get through the exercise in pairs.
- This was unusually long for a single technical exercise and created significant class-wide tension.

**Skills Developed**
- Listening.
- Patience.
- Emotional reaction.
- Physical presence.
- Comfort with silence.
- Nonverbal storytelling.
- Letting offers land.
- Avoiding panic-talk.
- Allowing subtext to develop.
- Scene weight and groundedness.

**Common Failure Modes**
- Players forget the delay and respond immediately.
- Players become visibly anxious during silence.
- Players treat the pause as dead air instead of playable time.
- Players count silently but stop acting while they count.
- Players fill the silence with nervous gestures instead of meaningful behavior.
- The exercise becomes about rule compliance instead of scene development.
- Players overcorrect and make the scene slow, frozen, or lifeless.
- The instructor resets the scene so often that students become afraid of the exercise.
- In a large class, excessive resets can make the exercise consume too much class time.

**Coaching Notes**
- The silence must stay alive.
- The goal is not merely to count to five; the goal is to let the previous offer affect the character.
- During the delay, players should do something playable:
  - react emotionally,
  - adjust physically,
  - look away or look closer,
  - handle an object,
  - change status,
  - make a decision,
  - reveal discomfort,
  - let the relationship breathe.
- A successful pause contains thought, feeling, or action.
- This exercise should probably not be run as a perfection test.
- It works better as a tolerance-building drill: players need time to experience silence without being punished for every mistake.
- A better progression may be:
  - demonstrate the exercise clearly,
  - run a short silent reaction drill,
  - try a three-second delay,
  - move to a five-second delay,
  - coach during the scene rather than constantly resetting,
  - limit each pair to a short rep.
- For a large class, strict resets can make the exercise consume too much time and create dread rather than skill growth.
- The goal is not obedience to a stopwatch; the goal is alive silence, listening, and playable reaction.

**Flying Pig Specific Notes**
- This exercise is used rarely.
- The last observed use was one of the biggest exercise failures Troy has seen in class.
- A class of roughly 14 students took about an hour to complete the exercise in pairs.
- Troy and Zero went first, and their scene was cut and reset repeatedly before the second line of dialogue was delivered.
- Jesse also cut and reset other pairs multiple times.
- Most students struggled to remember or maintain the five-second delay.
- The repeated resets made the class visibly afraid of the exercise.
- The exercise’s failure appeared to come less from the concept and more from the facilitation: too many hard resets, too little runway, and too much pressure on perfect compliance.
- Troy believes the exercise is still valuable and should be tried again, but with better containment and clearer progression.

### I've Got a Secret
**Type:** Two-Person Scene Exercise / Subtext Exercise / Character Constraint Exercise  
**Standard:** Unknown / Needs Review  
**Working Name:** I've Got a Secret  
**Observed In:** Advanced Class  
**Observed Frequency:** Observed once, during Troy's first advanced class  
**Status:** Partial documentation; name and exact standard-game identity unknown

**Purpose**
- Give each player a hidden want, wound, need, emotional condition, or private agenda.
- Practice letting internal information shape behavior without directly announcing it.
- Build subtext into a scene.
- Encourage players to reveal character through action, reaction, point of view, and emotional behavior.
- Train players to notice their partner's behavior and infer what is underneath it.
- Practice playing emotionally specific scenes where the deepest information is present but not expositional.

**Setup**
- Two players perform a scene.
- Before the scene begins, each player draws a secret from a box provided by the coach.
- Each player privately reads their own secret.
- Players do not show or tell their secret to their scene partner.
- The coach or audience provides a get for the scene.

**Documented Secret Examples**
- "Wants to make a friend."
- "Wants money."
- "Recently lost a child."
- "Holidays make me sad."

**Sequence of Play**
1. Two players are selected.
2. Each player draws one secret from the coach's box.
3. Each player privately reads their secret.
4. The players receive a get for the scene.
5. The players begin a two-person scene.
6. Each player uses their secret to inform their character, behavior, emotional state, point of view, or choices.
7. Players are not allowed to directly announce or explicitly reveal their secret.
8. The scene plays out normally, with the secrets operating as subtext.
9. At the end of the scene, each player is asked to guess the other player's secret.
10. The actual secrets may then be revealed.

**Rules**
- Do not directly state your secret.
- Do not turn the scene into interrogation or a guessing game during the scene.
- Let the secret affect what you want, how you listen, how you react, and what you avoid.
- The secret should influence the character without replacing the relationship, setting, or scene action.
- The scene still needs a clear who, what, and where.
- The secret should create playable subtext, not private mental trivia.

**Skills Developed**
- Subtext.
- Character want.
- Emotional point of view.
- Listening.
- Inference.
- Playing need without exposition.
- Revealing information through behavior.
- Partner observation.
- Two-person scene work.
- Justification.
- Emotional specificity.
- Relationship tension.

**Common Failure Modes**
- A player blurts out their secret.
- A player hints so obviously that the scene becomes a guessing game.
- A player hides the secret so completely that it has no effect.
- The secret overwhelms the relationship or scene premise.
- Players focus more on being guessed correctly than on playing a strong scene.
- The scene becomes vague because both players are privately protecting their secrets.
- Players forget to establish who, what, and where.
- Players play the secret intellectually instead of behaviorally.
- Players do not listen closely enough to infer their partner's secret.
- Heavy secrets are played melodramatically without relationship grounding.
- Light secrets are underplayed and fail to affect behavior.

**Coaching Notes**
- The secret should shape behavior, not become dialogue.
- A secret can create: need, grief, avoidance, urgency, insecurity, guilt, hope, desperation, emotional guardedness, or status behavior.
- The scene should remain playable even if nobody guesses the secret correctly.
- A successful scene does not require a perfect guess.
- The real goal is for the secret to create readable subtext and stronger character behavior.
- Players should avoid playing "I am hiding a secret" generically. The specific secret should change what they want and how they react.
- The partner's job is not to interrogate; the partner's job is to notice behavior while continuing the scene.
- Strong secret pairings can create unexpectedly powerful scenes when the private needs or wounds collide naturally.

**What Success Looks Like**
- Each character has a clear behavioral tilt from the secret.
- The scene still has a grounded relationship and situation.
- The secrets create tension, surprise, emotional specificity, or vulnerability.
- The players do not directly reveal their secrets.
- At the end, the guesses are at least directionally connected to what was played.
- The audience or class can see how the secret influenced the performance.
- The scene feels deeper because the characters are carrying private wants or wounds.

**Flying Pig Specific Notes**
- Troy remembers playing this once in his first advanced class.
- Jesse provided a box of secrets.
- Each player drew a secret before the scene.
- The players used the secrets to inform their characters.
- The players were not allowed to directly tell their secrets.
- After the scene, each player guessed their partner's secret.
- In one scene, the secrets were "Wants to make a friend" and "Wants money."
- In another scene, the randomly selected secrets were "Recently lost a child" and "Holidays make me sad." Troy remembers those as synergizing into a terrifyingly good scene.
- Exact official name, time limit, and full coaching emphasis are not yet documented.

- The player becomes self-conscious after a weak character.
- The player demonstrates quirks instead of playable characters.

**Coaching Notes**
- Each character needs an immediate visible difference.
- Strong changes may come from: posture, face, tempo, voice placement, emotional state, status, age, rhythm, physical center, or point of view.
- The exercise rewards boldness over nuance.
- A simple but committed character is better than a subtle underplayed one.
- Players should avoid making 10 versions of themselves with minor accents.
- The clearest strategy is to rotate major variables quickly: high status / low status, old / young, fast / slow, joyful / terrified / furious / heartbroken, grounded / frantic, open / secretive, powerful / needy.
- The character should be readable from the back of the room within one or two seconds.

**What Success Looks Like**
- The player reaches 10 characters within 60 seconds.
- The characters are clearly distinct.
- The player commits physically and vocally.
- The player uses a wide emotional and status range.
- The transitions are fast.
- The audience or auditors can immediately tell that a new character has appeared.
- The player does not apologize, explain, or collapse after weak choices.

### It's Tuesday
**Type:** Solo Emotional Range Exercise / Audition Exercise  
**Standard:** Unknown / Needs Review  
**Observed In:** Auditions  
**Observed Frequency:** Observed once  
**Status:** Partial documentation; core rule remembered clearly

**Purpose**
- Test emotional range under pressure.
- Practice generating large emotional responses from mundane material.
- Train players to make emotional shifts visible, immediate, and distinct.
- Encourage emotional commitment without needing a dramatic premise.
- Reveal whether a player can heighten internal stakes from ordinary circumstances.
- Practice sustaining a one-person scene through emotional progression.

**Setup**
- One player performs alone.
- The coach or teacher provides a very mundane get.
- The player must perform a one-person scene inspired by that get.
- During the scene, the player must move through three distinct big emotions.

**Documented Example Get**
- "I should mow my lawn sometime this week."

**Sequence of Play**
1. One player is selected.
2. The teacher gives the player a mundane statement or situation.
3. The player begins a solo scene or monologue.
4. The player talks themself through the situation.
5. Over the course of the scene, the player must demonstrate three distinct large emotional states.
6. The emotions should be clear, readable, and meaningfully different from each other.
7. The scene ends after the emotional progression has been demonstrated.

**Rules**
- The get should be mundane.
- The player performs alone.
- The player must demonstrate three big emotions.
- The emotions should be distinct, not slight variations of the same feeling.
- The player should not merely name the emotions.
- The emotional states should be performed through voice, body, face, rhythm, point of view, and behavior.
- The ordinary get should remain connected to the scene.

**Skills Developed**
- Emotional range.
- Emotional readability.
- Solo commitment.
- Heightening.
- Justification.
- Emotional transitions.
- Making mundane material playable.
- Vocal and physical expressiveness.
- Audition readiness.
- One-person scene structure.

**Common Failure Modes**
- The emotions are too small to read.
- The player changes the words but not the body or voice.
- The emotions are not distinct enough.
- The player abandons the mundane get instead of heightening it.
- The scene becomes a list of emotions rather than a playable progression.
- The player comments on the exercise instead of performing it.
- The player chooses emotionally restrained or ironic reactions.
- The player rushes through the emotions without letting each one land.
- The player cannot justify why an ordinary statement matters so much.

**Coaching Notes**
- The point is not realism; the point is emotional availability and readability.
- Mundane material is useful because it forces the player to supply the stakes.
- Strong emotional choices might include: panic, joy, grief, shame, awe, rage, desperation, heartbreak, ecstatic confidence, confusion, fear, or overwhelming relief.
- Each emotional shift should have a playable reason, even if the reason is heightened or absurd.
- The player should avoid subtle disappointment, detached irony, or intellectual commentary unless the exercise specifically allows restrained emotional modes.
- The strongest version makes the ordinary get feel theatrically important through the player's emotional life.

**What Success Looks Like**
- The player creates three clearly different emotional states.
- Each emotion is physically and vocally readable.
- The mundane get remains the anchor.
- The emotional progression feels heightened but playable.
- The player commits without apology.
- The scene has momentum rather than feeling like three disconnected demonstrations.
- The audience or auditors can easily identify the emotional shifts.

### Best Friends
**Type:** Short-form / Skills Exercise  
**Standard:** Flying Pig observed; exact standard status unknown  
**Observed In:** Class exercise  
**Observed Frequency:** Troy has practiced it once  
**Status:** Reviewed / Troy-described

**Purpose**
- Practice matching a partner's energy.
- Build strong scenic agreement between two players.
- Train emotional, physical, and vocal synchronization.
- Develop distinct characters who still share the same rhythm, intensity, and worldview.
- Practice contrast through a late opposing-energy entrance.
- Help players sustain a shared relationship dynamic under disruption.
- Build clear audience readability through unified energy.

**Setup**
- Two players begin the scene.
- The coach gives them a simple word suggestion.
- The first two players use the word to initiate a scene.
- The scene runs primarily as a two-person scene.
- The two players should play distinct characters while matching each other's emotional, physical, and vocal energy.
- A third player enters near the end with opposing energy.

**Sequence of Play**
1. Two players receive a word suggestion.
2. They begin a scene inspired by the word.
3. The two players establish a shared energy quickly.
4. The players should remain distinct characters, but their emotional tone, physicality, vocal rhythm, intensity, status style, and worldview should strongly match.
5. Most of the scene remains a two-person scene.
6. Near the end, a third player enters.
7. The third player brings energy that opposes both players' shared energy.
8. The contrast reveals or tests the relationship between the best friends.
9. The scene ends shortly after the third player's entrance, once the contrast has landed.

**Important Rules**
- The first two players are not literally required to be best friends, but they should feel strongly aligned.
- The two players should not copy each other exactly like a mirror exercise.
- They should remain distinct characters while sharing the same broad energy.
- The third player should oppose the shared energy of both players, not just one of them.
- The third player enters late and functions as a disruption or contrast, not as an equal-length third scene partner.
- The scene should end shortly after the third player's entrance, once the contrast clarifies the first two players' dynamic.

**Example From Troy**
- Troy and Adam received a word such as "Gym" or "Workout."
- They played high schoolers in gym class who were very invested in toxic masculinity.
- Their shared energy was performative bravado and all-talk masculinity.
- A third player entered as the coach.
- The coach demonstrated real masculinity.
- Troy and Adam's characters were revealed as all talk by contrast.
- The third player opposed both players' shared energy and helped expose the truth of the relationship.

**Skills Developed**
- Energy matching.
- Partner listening.
- Emotional synchronization.
- Vocal matching.
- Physical matching.
- Relationship alignment.
- Character contrast.
- Late-scene disruption.
- Shared worldview.
- Scenic unity.
- Audience-readable energy.
- Maintaining distinct characters inside a shared tone.

**Common Failure Modes**
- The first two players do not match energy clearly.
- The players copy each other mechanically instead of sharing a playable energy.
- The players become identical rather than distinct aligned characters.
- One player drives while the other follows passively.
- The shared energy is too subtle for the audience to read.
- The third player enters too early.
- The third player fails to oppose both players' energy.
- The third player becomes the new center of the scene instead of revealing the first two players.
- The scene continues too long after the third player enters.
- The exercise becomes a generic three-person scene instead of a two-person energy-matching exercise with a late contrast.

**Coaching Notes**
- The first two players should quickly identify and heighten a shared energy.
- Matching means emotional, physical, and vocal alignment, not identical behavior.
- Distinct characters can still share the same rhythm, intensity, attitude, and worldview.
- The third player should make a clean, contrasting offer.
- The third player's job is to expose or test the shared energy, not to steal the scene.
- Strong versions make the first two players look more unified after the third player appears.
- The scene should button soon after the contrast becomes clear.
- This exercise is useful for players who need to practice ensemble agreement without flattening character difference.

**What Success Looks Like**
- The first two players feel strongly connected.
- Their shared energy is readable quickly.
- The audience can tell why these two characters belong together.
- The characters remain distinct while sharing emotional/physical/vocal tone.
- The third player's entrance creates immediate contrast.
- The contrast reveals something about the first two players.
- The scene ends soon after the disruption lands.
- The exercise sharpens partner awareness and energy matching.

### Status Inversion
**Type:** Skills Exercise / Status Scene Drill  
**Standard:** Flying Pig observed; standard status-work concept  
**Common Names:** Status Inversion  
**Observed In:** Flying Pig class exercise with guest teacher  
**Status:** Active  

#### Purpose

- Practice status awareness.
- Explore power dynamics inside clear relationships.
- Train players to separate formal role from actual power.
- Make status behavior readable to the audience.
- Practice clear physical, vocal, and emotional choices that communicate dominance, submission, confidence, dependence, control, or insecurity.
- Help players understand how status can shift without changing the stated relationship.

#### Setup

- Two players are given an uneven relationship with an obvious social hierarchy.
- Example relationships:
  - teacher / student,
  - boss / employee,
  - parent / child,
  - doctor / patient,
  - coach / athlete,
  - celebrity / fan,
  - officer / civilian.
- The players begin a scene with the roles intact.
- At some point during the scene, the coach tells the players to reverse the status while staying in the scene.

#### Sequence of Play

1. Two players receive an uneven relationship.
2. The scene begins normally, with both players maintaining the assigned relationship.
3. The coach allows the scene to establish the relationship and initial power dynamic.
4. During the scene, the coach instructs the players to reverse the status.
5. The players continue the same scene without dropping character or changing the external roles.
6. The technically lower-status role now behaves as the higher-status person.
7. The technically higher-status role now behaves as the lower-status person.
8. The scene continues briefly, usually around 1–3 minutes total.
9. The rep ends once the inversion is clear and readable.

#### Important Rules

- Keep the external roles intact.
  - The teacher is still the teacher.
  - The student is still the student.
  - The boss is still the boss.
  - The employee is still the employee.
- Reverse the actual power, confidence, control, or status behavior.
- Do not solve the inversion by changing the premise or explaining it away.
- The inversion should happen while staying in the scene.
- Either grounded realism or heightened comedy is allowed.
- The most important goal is that the status inversion is obvious to the audience.

#### Skills Developed

- Status awareness.
- Power dynamics.
- Relationship clarity.
- Physical status behavior.
- Vocal status behavior.
- Emotional confidence.
- Submissiveness / dominance.
- Scene adjustment in real time.
- Playing against expected hierarchy.
- Audience readability.
- Staying in character through a coach-given shift.

#### Common Failure Modes

- Players change the external roles instead of inverting the status.
- The teacher stops being the teacher, or the boss stops being the boss.
- The inversion is too subtle for the audience to read.
- Players talk about status instead of behaving with status.
- The higher-status player only becomes louder rather than more powerful.
- The lower-status player only becomes quieter rather than more dependent or deferential.
- Players freeze when the coach calls for the reversal.
- The scene becomes an explanation of why the power changed instead of simply playing the new behavior.
- The players split the difference and both land in medium status.
- The status shift is visible for one line but not sustained.

#### Coaching Notes

- Status must be playable and visible.
- Make the inversion obvious to the audience.
- Use body, voice, space, eye contact, tempo, and permission-giving behavior.
- High status may show through calmness, stillness, entitlement, command, ease, certainty, or control of space.
- Low status may show through eagerness, apology, nervous movement, approval-seeking, hesitation, deference, or physical retreat.
- The roles should remain recognizable.
- The audience should be able to say who technically holds the role and who actually holds the power.
- The exercise can be grounded or comedic; clarity matters more than realism.
- The strongest versions make the power reversal visible without needing to explain it.

#### What Success Looks Like

- The assigned relationship remains clear.
- The formal hierarchy is recognizable.
- The actual status is visibly reversed.
- The audience can immediately perceive the inversion.
- The players continue the scene instead of stopping to discuss the exercise.
- Physical, vocal, and emotional behavior all support the status shift.
- The scene remains playable after the inversion.

#### Flying Pig Specific Notes

- Troy did this at Flying Pig with a guest teacher.
- The guest teacher emphasized making the inversion obvious to the audience.
- The coach gave the players an uneven relationship.
- At one point during the scene, the coach instructed the players to reverse the status while staying in scene.
- The external roles remained intact.
- Either grounded realism or heightened comedy was allowed.
- Reps were short, roughly 1–3 minutes.

### 1 Minute Fairy Tale
**Type:** Short Form Game  
**Standard:** Yes  
**Common Names:** 1 Minute Fairy Tale; Half Life; Half-Life  
**Observed In:** Shows / Short Form Sets / Practice  
**Player Count:** Typically 3-5 performers plus a host

**Purpose**
- Story compression.
- Editing.
- Physical shorthand.
- Ensemble coordination under time pressure.
- Preserving recognizable story beats while reducing time.
- Creating comedy through increasing compression and panic.

**Setup**
- A host introduces the game.
- The host gets an audience suggestion for a fairy tale or well-known story.
- The suggestion must be known by at least one of the performers.
- Players briefly plan before the first run.
- In practice, the planning period lasts until the players are ready, but the preference is to keep it quick.
- Much of the planning time may be spent with the player who knows the story explaining it to players who do not.

**Structure**
The players perform the same story repeatedly, with each version compressed into a shorter time limit:

- 60 seconds
- 30 seconds
- 15 seconds
- 7 seconds
- 3.5 seconds
- 1 second

The 1-second version is always the finale.

**Rules**
- Players act out the story in 60 seconds.
- The same performers then replay the same story in 30 seconds.
- The process continues through each shorter time limit.
- Players try to replay the same major story beats in increasingly compressed form.
- Later rounds should compress the original interpretation rather than becoming entirely new scenes.
- The host times each round and calls time.
- The host does not typically count down during the round.

**Host Responsibilities**
- Introduce and frame the game.
- Get a fairy tale or known story from the audience.
- Ensure at least one player knows the story well enough to explain it.
- Give players a short organizing period.
- Time each round.
- Call time when the round ends.
- Bring energy by treating each reduced time limit as a surprise or escalating challenge for the players.
- Run interference with the audience while the players quickly organize between rounds.
- Keep the game moving and prevent planning from dragging.

**Skills Developed**
- Story compression.
- Physical storytelling.
- Ensemble agreement.
- Fast planning.
- Clear story landmarks.
- Stage picture.
- Time awareness.
- Efficient character and plot shorthand.
- Commitment under increasing pressure.
- Recognizing which beats are essential.

**Common Failure Modes**
- Players take too long planning.
- Later rounds introduce new story beats instead of compressing the established version.
- Players lose the recognizable landmarks of the story.
- The group treats each round as a new scene.
- The story becomes physically unclear as it gets faster.
- Players panic and drop the main arc.
- The 1-second finale becomes random noise instead of a compressed button.

**Coaching Notes**
- The game depends on preserving recognizable story landmarks.
- Each shorter version should feel like the same story under more pressure.
- Physical shorthand becomes more important as time decreases.
- Big, readable character and plot gestures help the audience track the story.
- Players do not need a perfect literary retelling; they need a clear shared version that can be compressed.
- Sharla once noted that adding a very out-of-the-blue ending that does not match the fairy tale at all can be good.
- An absurdly divergent ending can create a strong laugh, especially when the audience understands the original story well enough to feel the break.

**Flying Pig Specific Notes**
- Flying Pig typically plays this with 3-5 performers plus a host.
- The audience gives a fairy tale or known story.
- At least one performer must know the story.
- Prep usually lasts until the players are ready, but the room prefers it to be quick.
- The player who knows the story often explains it rapidly to players who do not.
- Players replay the same beats in compressed form.
- The host times and calls time but does not count down.
- The host creates energy around each reduced time limit and fills audience-facing space while players organize.
- The 1-second version is always the finale.
- Troy wrote down Half Life as a game seen at Flying Pig in March.
- After reviewing the standard Half Life structure, Troy recognized that Flying Pig calls this structure 1 Minute Fairy Tale.
- The catalog keeps 1 Minute Fairy Tale as the primary Flying Pig name and preserves Half Life / Half-Life as alias terms.

### Why Were You Late?
**Type:** Guessing Game  
**Standard:** Yes  
**Common Names:** Late for Work; Why Were You Late?  
**Observed In:** Shows / Short Form Sets / Practice  
**Player Count:** Typically 4-5 players

**Purpose**
- Practice guessing out loud.
- Practice charades-based support.
- Train a guesser to stay in character while solving.
- Train the boss to guide without giving the answer away.
- Build audience engagement through near-misses and discoveries.
- Create comedy from escalating workplace interrogation and physical clues.

**Roles**
- **Guesser / Late Employee:** Leaves the room while the excuse is created, then returns and must guess why they were late.
- **Boss:** Interrogates the late employee, provides verbal hints, and steers the guessing.
- **Charades Players:** Stand behind or near the boss and physically act out the hidden clues.
- **Host:** May be separate from the boss or functionally handled by the boss, depending on staging.

**Audience Suggestion Structure**
- Flying Pig typically builds the excuse from three components.
- The host asks targeted audience questions that combine into a single explanation.
- The categories are not fixed, but often resemble:
  - a geographical location,
  - an obscure mode of transportation,
  - something unexpected that happened.
- Other prompt structures may be used if they produce a playable three-part excuse.

**Example Excuse Structure**
- Geographical location: Hutchinson.
- Mode of transportation: bicycle.
- Unexpected event: fell into a sinkhole.
- Final excuse: “I fell into a sinkhole while riding a bicycle through Hutchinson.”

Another possible structure:
- Item of clothing: socks.
- What was wrong with it: wrong color.
- Distraction: playing on my phone.
- Final excuse: “I wore the wrong color socks because I was playing on my phone.”

**Sequence of Play**
- The guesser leaves the room.
- The host or boss gets three audience suggestions and combines them into a late excuse.
- The guesser returns as the late employee.
- The boss interrogates the employee about being late.
- Charades players act out the hidden components behind or near the boss.
- The guesser guesses one component at a time.
- The audience snaps when the guesser is close.
- The audience claps when a component is correct.
- The game continues until the guesser identifies the full excuse or the host/boss ends the game.

**Audience Feedback**
- Snap = close.
- Clap = correct component.

**Boss Responsibilities**
- Keep the interrogation active.
- Give verbal hints without solving directly.
- Steer the guesser toward one missing component at a time.
- Maintain the boss/employee relationship.
- Keep the game moving if the guesser stalls.
- Watch the audience feedback and use it to guide the employee.
- Avoid making the answer too obvious too quickly.

**Boss Hint Examples**
- If the guesser has identified “socks” but not “wrong color,” the boss might say:
  - “You figured out the socks. But what could possibly be wrong with a pair of socks?”
- If the clue involves sinking, the boss might say:
  - “You are sinking in my respect.”

**Charades Player Responsibilities**
- Never stop moving.
- Make clues physically clear.
- Change tactics when the guesser is not understanding.
- Support the guesser without speaking.
- Use big, readable physical choices.
- If the boss looks over their shoulder, immediately pretend not to have been doing anything.

**Flying Pig Specific Notes**
- Flying Pig typically uses a three-component excuse.
- The guesser usually solves one component at a time.
- The boss can steer the guesser toward the next component after one has been identified.
- A recurring bit is that the boss occasionally looks over their shoulder, and the charades players immediately pretend they were not doing anything.
- The boss role is especially important in the Flying Pig version.
- Troy has played boss, guesser, and charades roles.
- Troy has received direct praise for playing the boss.
- Troy has overheard comments that he should always play the boss in this game.
- Derek, Bree, and David praised Troy’s ability to remain in character while guessing.
- Sharla’s general guessing-game note applies strongly here: keep guessing out loud; silence is failure, wrong guesses are useful, and the audience enjoys the struggle.

**Common Failure Modes**
- The guesser stops guessing.
- The charades players stop moving.
- The boss gives the answer away directly.
- The boss fails to steer the guesser toward the next missing component.
- Charades are too small or unclear.
- The guesser drops character and only solves mechanically.
- The audience feedback is ignored.
- The three components do not combine into a playable excuse.

**Coaching Notes**
- Guess constantly.
- Wrong guesses are part of the game.
- The boss should make the guesser feel supported while still under comic pressure.
- The boss should use verbal hints that narrow the search without solving it.
- Charades players should switch tactics when a clue path fails.
- The late employee should stay in character even while solving.
- The best versions maintain the workplace scene while still functioning as a guessing game.

**Success Looks Like**
- The guesser remains active and in character.
- The boss hints without solving.
- Charades stay physical and readable.
- The audience can follow each near-miss and discovery.
- Momentum never stalls.
- The final reconstructed excuse feels satisfying and funny.

### Freeze Tag
**Type:** Short Form Game  
**Standard:** Common variation; Flying Pig version replaces both frozen players  
**Observed In:** Shows / Short Form Sets / Practice  
**Player Count:** Usually about 6 players, but can scale larger

**Purpose**
- Practice initiation.
- Encourage physicality.
- Build rapid scene creation.
- Train players to justify physical positions immediately.
- Create strong visual stage pictures.
- Practice bold edits and clean scenic resets.

**Rules / Flying Pig Structure**
- Two players begin a scene.
- The offstage players watch for interesting physical positions.
- An offstage player calls: “Freeze!”
- Both onstage performers freeze immediately.
- The player who called “Freeze” and another offstage player enter the scene.
- The two new players replace both frozen performers.
- Each new player takes the exact physical position of one frozen performer.
- The original performers leave.
- The new players justify the frozen positions and begin a completely new scene.
- The new scene is unrelated to the previous scene except for the inherited physical picture.
- The pattern repeats.

**Important Flying Pig Rule**
- In the Flying Pig version documented here, the caller does not replace only one performer.
- Two new performers enter and replace both original performers.

**Skills Developed**
- Physical commitment.
- Visual inspiration.
- Fast initiation.
- Justification.
- Bold scene starts.
- Reading stage pictures.
- Entering decisively.
- Creating a new reality from body position.
- Avoiding talking-head scenes.

**Common Failure Modes**
- Players create weak or generic body positions.
- Scenes devolve into standing and talking.
- New players ignore the physical inspiration.
- New players take too long to justify the positions.
- Initiations are too vague.
- New scenes feel too similar to the previous scene.
- Players call “Freeze” without having a clear initiation.
- The new players fail to fully take the exact frozen positions.

**Coaching Notes**
- Strong physical pictures make the game work.
- Call “Freeze” when the stage picture gives you an idea.
- Take the position first; justify it through the new scene.
- The new scene should start immediately and distinctly.
- Physicality should inspire the premise, relationship, status, environment, or emotional state.
- Avoid using the frozen position as a quick gag only; make it the start of a playable scene.

**Flying Pig Specific Notes**
- Flying Pig usually plays this with about six players.
- When “Freeze” is called, two new players replace both frozen players.
- Players are encouraged to use large body positions and create interesting visual pictures.
- David is especially strong at this game.
- Kim is also strong at this game.
- Troy does okay.
- The game succeeds when players create strong physical pictures and justify them immediately.

**Success Looks Like**
- The audience immediately understands the new scene.
- The new scene is clearly distinct from the previous one.
- The frozen positions are honored and justified.
- Players make bold, physical choices.
- The game has speed, variety, and clean transitions.

### Story, Story, Die!
**Type:** Elimination Storytelling Game  
**Standard:** Yes  
**Observed In:** Shows / Short Form Sets / Practice

**Purpose**
- Practice listening.
- Build verbal agility.
- Train immediate continuation.
- Develop narrative momentum.
- Create comic pressure through elimination.
- Practice playful failure and theatrical death.

**Setup**
- One player serves as the director.
- Several storytellers stand in a line.
- The director gets or initiates a story prompt, depending on the performance setup.
- The storytellers collectively tell one story.

**Rules**
- The director points to one storyteller.
- That player immediately continues the story.
- At any moment, the director points to another player.
- The new player must continue instantly from the previous player’s exact place in the story.
- The story should continue smoothly across player switches.
- If a player hesitates, stutters, fails to continue immediately, or otherwise breaks the flow, the audience and/or director yells: “DIE!”
- The eliminated player asks the audience for a death suggestion.
- The eliminated player performs a death inspired by that suggestion.
- The eliminated player remains onstage as a corpse.
- The story continues with the remaining players.
- When only one storyteller remains, that player delivers the moral of the story.
- The audience votes whether the final survivor lives or dies.

**Director Responsibilities**
- Point clearly.
- Keep the story moving.
- Switch storytellers often enough to create pressure.
- Watch for hesitation, stutter, or failure to continue.
- Call or invite “DIE!” when a player fails.
- As player count shrinks, become more aggressive.
- The director can absolutely hunt kills.
- Manage the endgame and audience vote.

**Audience Interaction**
- Audience and/or director yells “DIE!” when a storyteller fails.
- Audience provides death suggestions for eliminated players.
- Audience votes whether the final survivor lives or dies.

**Skills Developed**
- Listening.
- Immediate verbal response.
- Narrative continuation.
- Focus under pressure.
- Accepting the previous player’s syntax and direction.
- Avoiding pre-planning.
- Performing a quick theatrical death.
- Staying engaged after elimination as part of the stage picture.

**Common Failure Modes**
- Hesitation.
- Stuttering.
- Restarting the sentence structure.
- Repeating the previous player’s final words before continuing.
- Planning ahead instead of listening.
- Breaking causality.
- Ignoring the exact point where the story was handed off.
- Death scenes that are too slow or disconnected from the suggestion.
- Director switches that are unclear or too soft.

**Coaching Notes**
- Continue immediately.
- Do not restart the sentence.
- Do not repeat the previous words as a stall tactic.
- Trust the first continuation that appears.
- The story does not need to be brilliant; it needs to keep moving.
- The director should increase pressure as the field narrows.
- Eliminated players should enjoy the death and then become part of the stage picture without stealing focus.

**Flying Pig Specific Notes**
- Jesse likes to include this game when she is selecting games for a show.
- The director can hunt kills, especially as the number of players shrinks.
- Troy is weak at this game and tends to repeat final words before continuing.
- Zero is strong at this game.
- The final player gives the moral, and the audience votes whether they live or die.

**Success Looks Like**
- The story continues seamlessly across players.
- Players pick up instantly.

### Four Square
**Type:** Scene Game  
**Standard:** Yes  
**Observed In:** Shows / Short Form Sets / Practice  
**Player Count:** 4 players plus a host

**Purpose**
- Practice relationship tracking.
- Build continuity across multiple scenes.
- Train context switching.
- Practice returning to prior scenes with energy.
- Develop memory for relationships, premises, and emotional states.
- Create comedy and momentum through rotation and contrast.

**Setup**
- Four players form a box or square on stage.
- Each side of the square represents a different two-person scene.
- A host introduces the game.
- The host gets an audience suggestion for each side of the box.
- The host rotates the box during setup so each pair/side receives its own suggestion.

**Rules**
- Once each side has a suggestion, the game begins.
- Only the side facing the audience is active.
- The active pair performs the scene associated with that side.
- At any time, the host may rotate the square.
- Common rotation calls include:
  - “One to the left.”
  - “One to the right.”
  - “Two to the left.”
  - “Two to the right.”
- When the square rotates, the newly front-facing side resumes its own scene.
- Each side preserves its own relationship, premise, and emotional history.
- Scenes often return after a time jump rather than resuming from the exact previous second.

**Host Responsibilities**
- Introduce and frame the game.
- Gather one suggestion for each side.
- Rotate the square during setup to establish all four scenes.
- Start with predictable rotations so players and audience learn the four scenes.
- Diverge and become less predictable as the game goes on.
- Switch scenes to create contrast, momentum, and payoff.
- Avoid letting any one scene stall too long.
- End the game on a strong moment or accumulated payoff.

**Skills Developed**
- Scene memory.
- Relationship tracking.
- Context switching.
- Continuity.
- Fast re-entry.
- Emotional recall.
- Time jumps.
- Ensemble awareness.
- Playing multiple separate realities.
- Maintaining distinct characters and relationships.

**Common Failure Modes**
- Losing track of which relationship belongs to which side.
- Bleeding information from one scene into another.
- Restarting a scene instead of continuing it.
- Returning to a scene and replaying the same emotional beat.
- Spending too much time re-establishing context.
- Scenes are not distinct enough from each other.
- Players forget what changed during a prior visit.
- Host rotations are too chaotic before scenes are established.
- Host rotations are too predictable after the game is underway.

**Coaching Notes**
- Each side needs a distinct relationship and emotional engine.
- When a scene returns, it should continue or advance, not reset.
- Time jumps can help the scene evolve.
- The audience should be able to immediately recognize which scene has returned.
- Emotional stakes and relationship clarity matter more than the rotation mechanic.
- Strong players re-enter the scene with confidence, as if no time has passed for the performers even if time has passed in the world.

**Flying Pig Specific Notes**
- Four Square is one of Troy’s favorite games.
- Flying Pig runs this with four players forming a box onstage plus a host.
- The host gets a separate audience get for each side of the box.
- The host gives rotation instructions such as “one to the left.”
- Once all four sides have suggestions, the game begins.
- The host starts with predictable rotations and then diverges as the game goes on.
- Each scene often includes a time jump from the last time it had focus.
- Jesse coaches this game mostly around relationships and emotions.
- Jesse does much less interrupting when the class practices this game.

**Success Looks Like**
- The audience can distinguish all four worlds.
- Relationships remain clear when revisited.
- Time jumps advance the relationship or situation.
- Players immediately know which scene they are in after a rotation.
- The host’s rotations create momentum and contrast.
- The game feels like four living scenes rather than four repeated premises.

### Three-Headed Expert
**Type:** Short Form Game  
**Standard:** Yes  
**Observed In:** Shows / Short Form Sets / Practice

**Purpose**

- Flying Pig does not normally get an audience suggestion for the expert’s field of expertise before the game begins.

**Rules / Sequence of Play**
- The host introduces the game.
- The host asks the expert their name.
- The three experts answer one word at a time.
- The host asks one or two introductory questions to demonstrate how the game works.
- The host then asks the audience for questions.
- Audience questions become the main source of material.
- The experts answer each question one word at a time.
- The host continues taking questions until the game reaches a strong ending or time.

**Host Responsibilities**
- Introduce and frame the game.
- Ask the expert their name.
- Ask one or two warm-up questions so the audience understands the format.
- Solicit audience questions.
- Choose questions that give the experts something playable.
- Keep answers from dragging too long.
- End answers and move to the next question when appropriate.
- Maintain the illusion that the expert is confident and authoritative.

**Expert Responsibilities**
- Answer one word at a time.
- Listen to the sentence being built.
- Speak confidently.
- Avoid trying to be individually funny at the expense of the shared answer.
- Support coherence when possible.
- Treat impossible questions as answerable.

**Audience Interaction**
- Audience questions are a major component.

- “People are talking about orbital data centers, but given that they are limited to radiative heat, how do you recommend they mitigate the excess thermal energy?”

**Skills Developed**
- One-word-at-a-time listening.
- Shared sentence construction.
- Confidence.
- Group mind.
- Host control.
- Audience interaction.
- Commitment to nonsense authority.
- Accepting technical or impossible prompts.
- Building coherence from uncertainty.

**Common Failure Modes**
- Experts try to be individually funny.
- Experts lack confidence.
- The answer becomes slow and timid.
- Players fight the grammar of the sentence.
- Host questions are too weak or too easy.
- Audience questions do not create enough pressure.
- The host lets answers drag past the useful point.
- Experts try to actually know the answer instead of committing to the expert frame.

**Coaching Notes**
- Confidence matters more than correctness.
- Comedy comes from the expert answering impossible questions with authority.
- The experts should sound like one mind, not three people competing.
- The host should help the audience understand what kinds of questions are useful.
- The host’s first one or two questions are important because they demonstrate the rhythm and permission structure.
- A good question does not need to be answerable. It needs to be playable.

**Flying Pig Specific Notes**

- The host asks one or two questions before going to the audience.
- Flying Pig does not usually get an expert field from the audience first.
- Bree has been observed hosting.

- Troy is a strong audience member because he can ask technical science or technology questions that performers onstage could never realistically answer.
- The best audience questions expose the gap between confident delivery and actual knowledge.

**Success Looks Like**
- The expert answers with confidence.
- The answer feels coherent enough to follow.
- The audience enjoys watching the experts handle impossible material.
- The host keeps rhythm and chooses questions well.

### The Dating Game
**Type:** Short Form Game  
**Standard:** Common Short Form / Guessing Game  
**Observed In:** Shows / Short Form Sets / Practice  
**Player Count:** One guesser, three contestants, and usually a host

**Purpose**
- Practice character-based hinting.
- Practice guessing from indirect clues.
- Train contestants to provide useful information while staying in character.
- Build audience engagement through delayed discovery.
- Practice host control in a structured guessing game.
- Create comedy from dating-show questions colliding with strange identities.

**Setup**
- One player is the guesser.
- Three players are contestants.
- The guesser leaves the room or otherwise cannot hear the identity assignments.
- While the guesser is out, the contestants are assigned identities from audience suggestions.
- The host gets the audience suggestions and helps clarify the identities.

**Identity Types**
Flying Pig tends to vary the three identities rather than making all three the same type.

Possible identity categories include:
- animals,
- professions,
- character attributes,
- fears,
- fantasy identities,
- historical or philosophical types,
- roles,
- situations,
- or other playable concepts.

**Example Identity Sets**
- Has not slept in three days.
- Forest nymph.
- Chemical engineer.

Another possible set:
- Has nightmares about clowns.
- Kid at Space Camp.
- Ancient Greek philosopher.

**Rules / Sequence of Play**
- The guesser leaves while the identities are assigned.
- The host gets three different audience-suggested identities.
- Each contestant receives one identity.
- The guesser returns and plays a dating-game-style host or bachelor/bachelorette figure.
- The guesser asks each contestant dating-game-style questions.
- The guesser usually asks each contestant about three to four questions.
- Contestants answer in character.
- Contestants should give helpful hints without completely giving away the answer.
- The guessing usually happens at the end, after the questions are complete.
- The guesser guesses one contestant at a time.
- If the guesser is not right on the identity, the contestant or host may give a hint.
- The game continues until the guesser identifies the contestants or the host ends the game.

**Guesser Responsibilities**
- Ask questions that give contestants room to reveal their identities.
- Listen for repeated clues.
- Track all three contestants.
- Use the dating-show frame rather than turning the game into a direct interrogation too early.
- At the end, guess each contestant one at a time.
- Accept hints when a guess is close but not exact.

**Contestant Responsibilities**
- Stay in character.
- Give helpful hints.
- Avoid giving the identity away immediately.
- Let the identity affect voice, posture, behavior, word choice, worldview, and dating answers.
- Use the questions to reveal the identity from multiple angles.
- Make the guesser’s job possible.
- Avoid being so obscure or withholding that the game stalls.

**Host Responsibilities**
- Introduce and frame the game.
- Send the guesser out.
- Get audience suggestions for the three identities.
- Assign or clarify the identities.
- Bring the guesser back.
- Keep the question sequence moving.
- Help manage the final guessing.
- Provide or permit hints if the guesser is close but not exact.
- End the game once the identities have been discovered or the guessing has run its useful course.

**Audience Interaction**
- The audience provides the contestants’ identities while the guesser is out.
- Unlike some Flying Pig guessing games, the audience does not usually provide active snapping/clapping feedback during the guesser’s process.
- Because the guessing happens at the end, the guesser does not receive audience hint feedback throughout the scene.
- Hints at the end come from the contestant or host when needed.

**Skills Developed**
- Character hinting.
- Guessing.
- Listening.
- Audience suggestion incorporation.
- Delayed reveal.
- Host control.
- Character commitment.
- Specificity.
- Balancing concealment and clarity.
- Maintaining a game frame while solving.

**Common Failure Modes**
- Contestants hide the identity too much.
- Contestants give away the identity immediately.
- Contestants answer generically instead of letting the identity shape the answer.
- The guesser asks questions that do not help distinguish the contestants.
- The guesser stops listening across contestants.
- All three identities feel too similar.
- The host allows unclear or unplayable audience suggestions.
- The final guesses become slow because no hints are offered when the guesser is close.
- Contestants treat the identity as trivia instead of a playable character.

**Coaching Notes**
- Contestants should be helpful but not obvious.
- The best hints are playable, not merely factual.
- Contestants should answer through worldview, behavior, metaphor, and emotional reaction.
- The guesser should ask questions that invite specificity:
  - “Where would you take me on our first date?”
  - “What is your biggest red flag?”
  - “What gift would you bring my parents?”
  - “What would ruin our honeymoon?”
- The host should choose or shape identities so each contestant has a distinct kind of clue path.
- The final guessing should be supported, not punitive.
- If the guesser is close, a hint from the contestant or host keeps the game moving.

**Flying Pig Specific Notes**
- Flying Pig assigns the three identities from audience suggestions while the guesser is out of the room.
- The three identities often vary by category rather than all being one type.
- The guesser usually asks each contestant three to four questions.
- Contestants should give helpful hints without completely giving the answer away.
- The guessing happens at the end.
- The guesser guesses one contestant at a time.
- If the guesser is not exactly right, the contestant or host gives a hint.
- Active audience feedback such as snapping or clapping is not central to this game because the guessing does not happen continuously throughout the scene.

**What Success Looks Like**
- Each contestant is distinct and playable.
- The answers are funny or revealing while still functioning as hints.
- The guesser has enough information to make reasonable guesses.
- The final guesses feel earned.
- Hints help close gaps without dragging the ending.


### Job Interview
**Type:** Short Form Guessing Game  
**Standard:** Unknown / Common guessing-game family  
**Common Names:** Job Interview  
**Observed In:** Shows / Short Form Sets  
**Observed Frequency:** Seen once

**Purpose**
- Practice guessing out loud.
- Practice giving useful hints while staying in character.
- Build a playable interview scene around hidden information.
- Train players to support a guesser without giving away the answer too directly.
- Use audience feedback to guide discovery.
- Create comedy from the mismatch between an ordinary interview frame and absurd job conditions.

**Setup**
- One player leaves the room and becomes the guesser / job applicant.
- A host gets three audience suggestions:
  - a job,
  - the place where the job takes place,
  - an odd uniform.
- Three players sit in chairs as interviewers.
- The interviewers know the hidden job, location, and uniform.
- The guesser returns and is greeted by the host.
- The host tells the returning player that they are being interviewed by the seated interviewers.

**Observed Example**
- Job: Nap Coordinator.
- Place: Prison.
- Odd uniform: Space Suit.

**Sequence of Play**
1. The guesser leaves the room.
2. The host gathers the three hidden components from the audience.
3. The three interviewers take their seats and understand the hidden answers.
4. The guesser returns as the job applicant.
5. The host frames the situation as a job interview.
6. The interviewers ask the applicant a series of questions.
7. The questions contain hints about the job, the workplace/location, and the odd uniform.
8. The guesser makes guesses during the interview.
9. The audience snaps when the guesser is close.
10. The audience claps when the guesser gets one component correct.
11. The game continues until the guesser identifies the job, location, and uniform, or until the host ends the game.

**Audience Feedback**
- Snaps = close.
- Claps = correct component.

**Interviewer Responsibilities**
- Ask questions that sound like plausible job-interview questions.
- Hide clues inside the questions rather than simply stating the answers.
- Stay in the interview frame.
- Support the guesser with clearer hints if they are stuck.
- Use the applicant’s guesses as information about what clues are landing.
- Avoid asking questions so vague that the guesser has no path forward.
- Avoid making the answer too obvious too early.

**Guesser / Applicant Responsibilities**
- Stay in the job-applicant frame.
- Guess out loud.
- Listen to audience snaps and claps.
- Use wrong guesses to narrow the search.
- Treat interview questions as clues.
- Keep the scene alive even while solving.
- Once a component is confirmed, use it to infer the remaining components.

**Host Responsibilities**
- Introduce and frame the game.
- Send the guesser out of the room.
- Gather and clarify the three audience suggestions.
- Bring the guesser back.
- Explain the interview setup.
- Watch audience feedback and the guesser’s progress.
- Keep the game moving if the guessing stalls.
- End the game once the components are guessed or the scene has reached a practical endpoint.

**Skills Developed**
- Guessing.
- Hint-giving.
- Listening.
- Character commitment.
- Audience-feedback tracking.
- Interview framing.
- Supportive questioning.
- Maintaining a scene inside a guessing game.
- Justifying strange audience suggestions.

**Common Failure Modes**
- The guesser stops guessing out loud.
- Interviewers give clues that are too subtle.
- Interviewers give away the answers directly.
- Questions stop sounding like interview questions.
- The scene frame collapses into pure clue delivery.
- Audience snaps and claps are ignored.
- The guesser identifies one component but fails to use it to infer the others.
- Interviewers all hint at the same component while neglecting the remaining hidden information.
- The host lets the game stall too long.

**Coaching Notes**
- Wrong guesses are useful.
- The guesser should keep talking and guessing.
- Interviewers should ask questions that are both characterful and clue-bearing.
- Good questions can hint through job duties, workplace dangers, uniform requirements, training, schedule, coworkers, HR policies, or performance expectations.
- The game works best when the interview still feels like a real interview, even though the hidden job combination is absurd.
- Interviewers should distribute clues across all three hidden components.
- The host can rescue by asking the interviewers to clarify, by encouraging another question, or by nudging the applicant to think about one component at a time.

  - job,

  - odd uniform.
- The observed example was Nap Coordinator in a Prison wearing a Space Suit.

- The audience used snaps for close guesses and claps for correct components, consistent with other Flying Pig guessing games.
- The game was fun in the observed performance.

**What Success Looks Like**
- The applicant keeps guessing and remains in character.
- Interviewers give playable, progressively clearer clues.
- The audience understands the hidden components and enjoys the near-misses.
- Snaps and claps guide the solve.
- The final guesses feel earned rather than handed to the guesser.
- The interview frame stays intact until the end.

### Detective
**Type:** Short Form Guessing Game  
**Standard:** Unknown / Flying Pig observed guessing game  
**Observed In:** Flying Pig shows  
**Observed Frequency:** Troy has seen it performed at Flying Pig 2–3 times  
**Status:** Reviewed / Troy-described; not yet personally practiced by Troy

**Purpose**
- Practice nonverbal clue-giving.
- Practice gibberish communication with clear physical specificity.
- Train players to support a guesser without using English.
- Build audience enjoyment through information drift.
- Create a theatrical relay where each transmission becomes less precise and more chaotic.
- Develop bold mime, clear object work, and confident physical storytelling.
- Practice revealing and comparing distorted guesses.

**Setup**
- Six players are selected.
- A chair is placed in the middle of the stage.
- The chair represents a corpse.
- Five of the six players leave the room or otherwise cannot hear the setup.
- The one player who remains receives three pieces of information:
  - the corpse's profession,
  - the location of the murder,
  - the method of murder.
- The audience provides or helps provide these suggestions.
- The player who remains functions as the host / starting detective.

**Sequence of Play**
1. The starting player remains onstage with the chair representing the corpse.
2. The first returning player enters.
3. The two players communicate entirely in gibberish and mime.
4. The returning player knows they are trying to learn three things: the profession, the location, and the murder method.
5. The conversation should feel like two detectives investigating and discussing the case, except no English is used.
6. Either player may initiate clues. The previous player may actively give clues. The newly arrived player may ask questions through gibberish and mime. The exchange should feel like a natural conversation, not a rigid interrogation.
7. When the newly arrived player thinks they understand the profession, location, and method, they abruptly stage-murders the previous player.
8. The murdered player exits, dies, or otherwise leaves the active exchange.
9. The next player enters.
10. The newly informed player now becomes the clue-giver for the next player.
11. This relay continues until all five returning players have entered and received the information through gibberish and mime.
12. At the end, all players line up for the reveal.
13. The reveal proceeds by category: first, everyone states what they thought the profession was; then everyone states what they thought the location was; then everyone states what they thought the murder method was.
14. The reveal begins with the final player and works backward toward the starting player.

**Important Rules**
- All clue transmission during the relay is done in gibberish and mime.
- Players may not use English during the clue-passing phase.
- The incoming player knows the three target categories.
- The chair represents the corpse throughout the game.
- The abrupt stage murder is only a transition device.
- The transition murder does not need to match the actual guessed murder method.
- Any abrupt stage murder is acceptable as long as it clearly ends the exchange and passes the game to the next player.

**Skills Developed**
- Gibberish communication.
- Mime.
- Object work.
- Physical specificity.
- Guessing-game support.
- Nonverbal questioning.
- Nonverbal clue-giving.
- Category tracking.
- Memory under distortion.
- Audience-readable physical storytelling.
- Commitment to theatrical violence as a clean transition.
- Playing confidently without verbal precision.

**Common Failure Modes**
- Clues are too vague physically.
- Players rely on gibberish tone without enough mime.
- The clue-giver forgets one of the three categories.
- The incoming player does not ask enough clear physical questions.
- The exchange becomes passive instead of an active detective conversation.
- Players try to communicate through near-English sounds.
- The chair/corpse is forgotten after the setup.
- Players spend too long trying to get perfect certainty.
- The stage murder transition is unclear or too timid.
- The reveal becomes disorganized if players do not answer by category.
- Players reveal in player order instead of working backward from the final player.
- The game loses energy if the relay is treated as a puzzle rather than a performance.

**Coaching Notes**
- The game needs bold, readable physical offers.
- Gibberish alone is not enough; the mime must carry the information.
- Players should use the full stage picture, including the chair/corpse.
- Incoming players should actively investigate through gibberish questions and physical guesses.
- Clue-givers should respond as if they understand the detective's questions, even though the language is nonsense.
- The scene should feel like a heightened detective investigation, not like silent charades with noise.
- The murder transition should be sudden and clear.
- Wrong guesses are part of the entertainment; players should not overwork the clue transfer until it becomes slow.
- The final reveal should emphasize the drift from the original information through each relay step.

**What Success Looks Like**
- The audience can follow the broad intent of the mimed clues.
- The players commit fully to gibberish as meaningful language.
- Each exchange feels like a real detective conversation.
- The information mutates in funny, trackable ways.
- The stage murders are clear and theatrical.
- The final reveal produces escalating comic payoff as the guesses differ from the original setup.
- The game remains fast enough to feel like a show piece rather than a classroom puzzle.

**Flying Pig Specific Notes**
- Troy has seen Detective performed at Flying Pig 2–3 times.
- Troy has not personally practiced it yet but thinks Flying Pig / his group should practice it.
- The game is best classified as advanced and show-ready.
- The host role is not a separate outside host in the usual sense; the first player who stays in the room effectively serves as the setup holder and first clue-giver.
- The game belongs with the catalog's short-form guessing games.

### Key to the City
**Type:** Short Form Guessing Game  
**Standard:** Unknown / Flying Pig observed guessing game  
**Observed In:** Flying Pig show or class context / needs exact source review  
**Status:** Reviewed / Troy-described

**Purpose**
- Practice guessing-game support.
- Practice subtle clue-giving through questions.
- Build a strong public press-conference frame.
- Train players to give useful clues without becoming too obvious too early.
- Practice character identity through reporter questions.
- Use audience feedback to guide the guesser.
- Create a comic heroic reveal through gradual discovery.

**Setup**
- Six players are selected.
- One player is the superhero and guesser.
- The superhero leaves the room or otherwise cannot hear the audience suggestions.
- One player remains as the press secretary / presenter.
- The remaining four players are reporters.
- The press secretary gets three audience suggestions:
  - the superhero's name,
  - the superhero's power,
  - the disaster the superhero saved the city from.
- The superhero is called back in.
- The scene is framed as a press conference where the city is honoring the superhero after the disaster.

**Roles**
- **Superhero / Guesser**: Returns to the press conference and must infer their superhero name, power, and the disaster they saved the city from.
- **Press Secretary / Presenter**: Runs the press conference, interviews the superhero, controls the structure, and helps guide the guessing.
- **Reporters**: Ask clue-bearing questions. Each reporter may establish their own reporter identity as they ask questions.

**Sequence of Play**
1. The superhero leaves the room.
2. The press secretary gathers three audience suggestions: superhero name, superhero power, disaster saved from.
3. The superhero returns.
4. The press secretary introduces or welcomes the superhero to the press conference.
5. The press secretary begins interviewing the superhero with questions that contain subtle clues.
6. Reporters ask questions from the audience/press pool.
7. Each reporter may set their own identity, outlet, attitude, or character through the way they ask their question.
8. The superhero guesses during the interview.
9. When the superhero says something close to one of the target answers, the audience snaps.
10. When the superhero correctly identifies one of the target answers, the audience claps.
11. The press secretary and reporters continue asking clue-bearing questions until the superhero identifies: superhero name, superhero power, and disaster saved from.
12. The game ends when all three pieces have been guessed or when the press secretary finds a clean button after the reveal.

**Important Rules**
- The superhero should guess out loud during the interview.
- The press secretary and reporters should give clues through questions, not direct answers.
- Audience feedback is part of the game: snapping means close, clapping means correct.
- Reporters are not assigned identities in advance; they create their own identities as they ask questions.
- The press conference frame should stay intact.
- Clues should be helpful but not immediately blunt.

### Sit, Stand, Lay
**Type:** Short Form Game  
**Standard:** Yes  
**Common Names:** Sit, Stand, Lie; Sit, Stand, Lay; Sit, Stand, Kneel/Lie Down

  - one player must be standing,
  - one player must be laying down.

- Physical awareness.
- Fast adjustment to partner choices.
- Justification of movement.
- Ensemble listening.
- Nonverbal support.
- Stage picture.

- Players change position without justifying it in the scene.
- The scene becomes only about the mechanics instead of the relationship or situation.
- Players watch the rule too tightly and stop listening emotionally.
- One player drives all the movement while the others passively react.
- Lying down becomes awkward or underused because players avoid the vulnerability of the position.
- Players create unclear intermediate positions that make it hard to tell whether someone is sitting, standing, or laying down.

### Home Shopping Network / HSN
**Type:** Short Form Game  
**Standard:** Common Variation / Guessing Game  
**Common Names:** Home Shopping Network; HSN; Home Shopping  
**Observed In:** Shows / Short Form Sets

**Purpose**
- Practice guessing out loud under pressure.
- Practice charades-based support.
- Build audience engagement through near-misses and discoveries.
- Train players to communicate clearly without words.
- Develop teamwork between clue-givers and guessers.
- Create comedy from salesmanship, confusion, and escalating product descriptions.
- Practice producer-led pacing and rescue in a guessing game.

**Rules**
- Five players participate.
- One player acts as the producer or game introducer.
- Two players are the home shopping hosts.
- Two players are interns who will silently assist the hosts.
- The producer introduces the game and sends the two host players out of the room.
- The producer then gets audience suggestions for three products.
- Each product has:
  - an item,
  - a unique ability or characteristic,
  - a celebrity endorser.
- The host players are called back into the room.
- The producer tells the hosts that the teleprompters are down, but the two interns will help them with their lines.
- The show then goes live with a countdown: “3…2…1.”
- The interns play charades to help the hosts guess what they are selling.
- The producer calls “Camera 1” or “Camera 2” to determine which intern is actively giving charades clues.
- The producer may switch cameras to change clue-givers, create rhythm, or rescue a stalled clue path.
- The hosts attempt to identify each item, its unique ability or characteristic, and its celebrity endorser.
- The audience snaps when the hosts are close.
- The audience claps when the hosts correctly guess something.
- If the hosts are stuck, the producer may “go to commercial” and give the hosts a verbal tip.
- The game continues until the hosts identify the required information or the producer ends the game.

**Producer Role**
- Introduces and frames the game for the audience.
- Sends the hosts out before suggestions are gathered.
- Gets and clarifies audience suggestions.
- Tracks the three products, including item, characteristic, and celebrity endorser.
- Brings the hosts back and explains the broken-teleprompter premise.
- Starts the broadcast with the countdown.
- Calls “Camera 1” and “Camera 2” to switch which intern is giving charades clues.
- Controls pacing by changing cameras when a clue path stalls.
- Watches the hosts’ progress and audience feedback.
- Uses “commercial break” interventions to give verbal tips when necessary.
- Helps keep the game moving without simply giving away the answer.
- Ends or transitions the game when the guessing sequence has completed or reached a natural stopping point.

**Skills Developed**
- Guessing out loud.
- Physical communication.
- Charades.
- Specificity.
- Listening to audience feedback.
- Salesmanship.
- Justification.
- Maintaining character while solving.
- Supporting partners under pressure.
- Escalating product pitches from partial information.
- Producer-led pacing.
- Rescue technique in structured short form.
- Switching between clue-givers.

**Common Failure Modes**
- Hosts stop guessing and wait silently for perfect clues.
- Interns repeat unclear charades instead of changing tactics.
- Hosts fail to use audience snaps and claps as feedback.
- Players get stuck on one part of the product and lose momentum.
- Interns act out the item but not the unique characteristic or celebrity endorser.
- Hosts guess too narrowly too early instead of using broad category guesses.
- The game becomes slow if the hosts are afraid of being wrong.
- Interns become frantic and physically unclear.
- The producer forgets to switch cameras, leaving one intern trapped in a failing clue path.
- The producer goes to commercial too late, after the audience has already felt the stall.
- The producer gives too much away during a commercial break.
- The scene frame collapses and players stop behaving like a home shopping broadcast.
- The products become too complicated for the hosts to reasonably guess.

**Coaching Notes**
- Silence is failure; wrong guesses are part of the game.
- Hosts should guess constantly and broadly:
  - category,
  - size,
  - material,
  - function,
  - setting,
  - emotion,
  - celebrity type,
  - famous roles,
  - rhyming or sound-alike clues.
- Interns should change tactics when a clue does not work.
- Charades should be large, specific, and repeated only when it is clearly helping.
- Hosts should remain cheerful, promotional, and committed to the broadcast even while confused.
- The home shopping frame gives the hosts permission to over-sell partial guesses.
- Audience snaps and claps are part of the information system; players should listen to them.
- The producer should use camera switches actively, not passively.
- Camera switches should help manage rhythm, reset unclear charades, and distribute support between the interns.
- Commercial breaks should be used as rescue tools, not as the main guessing mechanism.
- The producer should give tips that narrow the search without solving the answer for the hosts.
- The best versions keep the energy of a real sales pitch while the hosts slowly discover the truth.
- Interns should separate the clue targets: item, characteristic, and celebrity endorser.
- The producer should choose audience suggestions that are specific and playable, but not impossible.
- The producer needs judgment, not just confidence.
- A strong producer watches four things at once:
  - whether the hosts are still guessing,
  - whether the interns’ charades are readable,
  - whether the audience is still engaged,
  - whether the current clue path needs a camera switch or commercial break.
- Weak producers either under-intervene and let the game stall, or over-intervene and rob the hosts of the discovery.
- The best producer keeps the game moving while preserving the illusion that the hosts are figuring it out live.

**Flying Pig Specific Notes**

- One player serves as producer and introduces the game.

### Master Debater
**Type:** Short Form Game  
**Standard:** Common Short Form / Possible House Variant  
**Common Names:** Master Debater; Debate; Objection  
**Observed In:** Advanced Practice / Student Practice / Shows  
**Performance Status:** Confirmed public show use; Troy has performed it in practice

**Purpose**
- Practice fast verbal justification.
- Practice committing to absurd arguments.
- Build confidence speaking with authority.
- Encourage players to follow tangents and justify them.
- Create comedy from debate logic, rhetorical escalation, and objection-based pivots.
- Train players to listen closely for objection opportunities.

**Rules**
- One player serves as the judge.
- The other players stand in a line.
- The judge introduces the game and asks the audience for a topic.
- One player steps forward and begins making a case on the topic.
- At any point, another player in the line may step forward and say, “Objection!”
- The objecting player then objects to something the current speaker just said.
- The objection may be directly related to the topic or may be a tangent inspired by a word, phrase, implication, or detail.
- The judge rules on the objection by saying either:
  - “Sustained,” or
  - “Overruled.”
- If the objection is overruled, the current speaker continues.
- If the objection is sustained, the objecting player takes over and must continue arguing the point made in the objection.
- The new speaker continues until another player objects.
- The game continues through a series of arguments, objections, rulings, and takeovers until the judge ends the game.

**Judge Role**
- Introduces and frames the game.
- Gets the audience suggestion.
- Starts the first speaker.
- Rules quickly on objections.
- Controls rhythm and fairness.
- Rewards useful pivots, strong arguments, and playable tangents.
- Prevents the game from bogging down in unclear objections.
- Ends the game when it reaches a strong button or natural conclusion.

**Skills Developed**
- Verbal agility.
- Rhetorical confidence.
- Justification.
- Listening for hooks.
- Tangent recognition.
- Fast initiation.
- Status play.
- Commitment to weak logic.
- Persuasive nonsense.
- Recovery under pressure.
- Ensemble timing.
- Judge-led pacing.

**Common Failure Modes**
- Players wait too long to object.
- Players object without a clear playable angle.
- Objections become too logical and not theatrical enough.
- Speakers become vague instead of making strong claims.
- The judge rules too slowly and kills momentum.
- The judge sustains weak objections that do not create a playable direction.
- The judge overrules too often and prevents the game from changing hands.
- Players argue realistically instead of heightening the absurd debate frame.
- Tangents become disconnected noise instead of a new argument to commit to.
- Players abandon the debate tone and start casual banter.
- One strong speaker dominates because others hesitate to object.
- Players break character or go meta when they cannot find a strong objection or argument.
- Players comment on the difficulty of the game instead of sustaining the debate frame.
- The game collapses into self-consciousness if players treat weak objections as failures rather than playable offers.

**Coaching Notes**
- The speaker should make bold claims quickly.
- The objector should object to something specific, even if the objection is ridiculous.
- A good objection creates a new playable argument, not just a correction.
- The judge should rule fast.
- “Sustained” should usually be used when the objection creates better momentum.
- “Overruled” should be used when the objection is unclear, weak, or would derail a stronger current run.
- The game rewards confidence more than legal accuracy.
- Tangents are useful when they are committed to as if they matter.

### Spelling Bee
**Type:** Short Form Game  
**Standard:** Common Short Form / Flying Pig Variant  
**Common Names:** Spelling Bee; Improvised Spelling Bee  
**Observed In:** Practice / Shows  
**Observed Frequency:** Common in practice; occasional in shows

**Purpose**
- Practice fast group coordination.
- Practice one-word-at-a-time team speaking.
- Build character under a technical constraint.
- Create comedy from spelling pressure, team identity, and escalating word difficulty.
- Train players to stay in character while handling rules, rhythm, and mistakes.
- Give the host a strong facilitation role with room for heightening.

**Rules**
- Seven players participate.
- One player serves as the host.
- The remaining six players form two teams of three.
- The host introduces the game.
- The host usually establishes a setting or context for the spelling bee, such as:
  - fourth grade,
  - a senior center,
  - prison,
  - or another specific environment.
- The host asks each team to introduce themselves.
- Each team introduces itself one word at a time.
- The host asks one team to spell a word.
- The team repeats the word, claps, spells it one letter at a time, says the word again, and claps.
- The host then usually asks a follow-up question, such as:
  - “Can you use the word in a sentence?”
  - “Can you give me the definition?”
  - “Can you give me the etymology?”
- The host alternates or manages turns between the two teams.
- The words usually escalate in difficulty:
  - simple real words, such as “banana,”
  - harder real words, such as “existential,”
  - invented or absurd words, such as “Henryrodiniasm.”
- The game continues until the host ends it, a team reaches a strong comedic button, or the format culminates.

**Team Speaking Pattern**
- Team introductions are done one word at a time.
- Spelling is done one letter at a time.
- Follow-up answers may also use shared-team speaking, depending on how the game is being run.
- The team should sound like one shared character or shared brain while still preserving individual character behavior.

**Host Role**
- Introduces and frames the game.
- Establishes or reinforces the setting.
- Gets teams introduced.
- Chooses or provides words for the teams to spell.
- Escalates word difficulty over time.
- Asks follow-up questions after spellings.
- Controls pacing.
- Helps preserve the spelling bee frame.
- Creates opportunities for character, not just spelling.
- Ends the game at a strong moment.

**Skills Developed**
- One-word-at-a-time coordination.
- Group rhythm.
- Listening.
- Spelling under pressure.
- Fast recovery.
- Character commitment.
- Hosting.
- Escalation.
- Justification of absurd words.
- Team identity.
- Maintaining scene energy inside a technical structure.
- Playing quickly without abandoning clarity.

**Common Failure Modes**
- Teams slow down too much while trying to spell correctly.
- Players focus only on letters and lose character.
- The host lets the rhythm drag.
- The host gives words that are too hard too early.
- Players break or comment on mistakes instead of incorporating them.
- The team introduction becomes generic and does not establish a playable identity.
- Follow-up questions become administrative instead of heightening character or world.
- Players treat invented words as nonsense only, instead of using them to reveal character.
- Teams fail to clap or repeat the word cleanly, weakening the recognizable spelling bee structure.
- The setting disappears after the introduction.
- Players prioritize correctness over speed, confidence, and character.

**Coaching Notes**
- The goal is to go fast and stay in character.
- Correct spelling matters less than confident shared commitment.
- The host should use word difficulty as escalation.
- Early words should let the audience understand the pattern.
- Harder and invented words should increase pressure and absurdity.
- Follow-up questions should reveal character, team dynamics, or the setting.
- Teams should treat mistakes as gifts.
- One-word-at-a-time introductions should establish a clear team identity quickly.
- The host should avoid overexplaining and keep the game moving.

### Pillars
**Type:** Short Form Game  
**Standard:** Yes  
**Common Names:** Pillars; Human Props; Audience Pillars  
**Observed In:** Shows / Short Form Sets

**Purpose**
- Incorporate audience participation directly into a scene.
- Practice justifying unexpected words or phrases.
- Train players to make audience volunteers look good.
- Build flexibility inside a normal two-person scene.
- Create comedy from surprise insertions without abandoning scene reality.

**Rules**
- Two audience members are brought onstage.
- Each audience member is assigned to one player in the scene.
- The players begin a normal improvised scene.
- During the scene, a player may pause mid-sentence and tap their assigned audience member.
- The audience member supplies a word or short phrase.
- The player immediately incorporates that word or phrase into the sentence.
- The player then justifies the offer and continues the scene.
- The scene continues with players periodically using their pillars for inserted words or phrases.

**Example**
- Player: “I’m sorry I was late, I had to clean my…”
- Player taps pillar.
- Pillar: “Cat.”
- Player: “My cat. He was so dirty. Too much catnip.”

**Core Performance Principle**
- Pillars is a performer-struggle game, not a volunteer-struggle game. The audience member supplies the offer; the improviser carries the responsibility for making the offer work.

**Skills Developed**
- Justification.
- Audience interaction.
- Acceptance.
- Fast incorporation.
- Scene continuity.
- Making partners look good.
- Maintaining character under disruption.
- Listening.
- Flexibility.
- Recovery from unexpected offers.

**Common Failure Modes**
- Players use the pillars too often and prevent the scene from developing.
- Players use the pillars too rarely, making the audience participation feel irrelevant.
- Players ask for words in places where no answer could reasonably fit.
- Players reject, mock, or comment negatively on the audience member’s offer.
- Players treat the inserted word as a joke instead of making it true.
- The scene becomes only about the random words.
- Audience members give long, unclear, or deliberately disruptive answers.
- Players fail to protect nervous audience volunteers.
- The scene loses relationship, location, or stakes because the mechanic takes over.
- Players tap the pillar without setting up a clear blank.
- Players finish the sentence and then tap, leaving the audience member unsure what kind of word or phrase is needed.
- Players treat the volunteer as the source of the comedy instead of taking responsibility for the justification.
- Audience volunteers give overly obvious answers, which can reduce surprise but still must be accepted and justified.

**Coaching Notes**
- The scene still needs to be a real scene.
- The audience member’s answer is always right.
- The player’s job is to make the pillar look clever, not to show how hard the offer was.
- Ask for pillar input at moments where almost any noun, adjective, verb, name, or place could work.
- Strong players leave clear blanks:
  - “I brought you a…”,
  - “We can’t go back to…”,
  - “The doctor said I have too much…”,
  - “I knew this marriage was doomed when you bought the…”.
- Avoid overusing the pillar for every punchline.
- Use the inserted word to affect the relationship, not just decorate the sentence.
- If the audience member gives a strange answer, justify it with confidence and move forward.
- The best versions make the audience volunteer feel safe, funny, and included.
- The game creates difficulty for the performers, not for the audience volunteers.
- The performer should absorb the awkwardness and make the volunteer’s offer look intentional, useful, or funny.
- A good pillar prompt leaves a clear blank before the tap.
- Tapping with no lead-in gives the volunteer no useful frame.
- Tapping after the sentence is already complete creates confusion because the volunteer is not filling a meaningful gap.
- The player should set the pillar up to succeed, then justify whatever they provide.

**Flying Pig Specific Notes**
- Flying Pig runs this with two audience members brought up as pillars.
- Each audience member is assigned to one scene player.
- Players tap their assigned pillar when they want the audience member to fill in part of a sentence.
- The player then incorporates and justifies the supplied word or phrase.
- The game rewards acceptance, justification, audience care, and scene stability.
- It aligns with Flying Pig’s broader emphasis on accepting gifts and making scene partners look good.
- Alan struggles with this game.
- Kim also struggles somewhat and may tap a pillar with no lead-in or after completing a sentence.
- Troy is comfortable with this game.
- Derek is good as an onstage player in this game.
- In practice, Derek has sometimes served as the pillar rather than the player and struggles in that role because he tends to provide the most obvious or basic fill-in for the sentence.
- The game highlights two separate skills: player-side justification and volunteer-side offer generation.
- In public performance, the volunteer-side skill cannot be assumed, so the onstage player must be ready to make even basic or awkward answers work.

**Post-V4 Reconciliation Additions**
- Common player mistakes:
  - Triggering too early.
  - Triggering too late.
  - Completing the sentence before tapping.
- Common pillar mistakes:
  - Slow response.
  - Conventional response.
- Success resembles Lines From a Box:
  - Play a real scene.
  - Integrate the input naturally.

### 3 Chairs
**Type:** Short Form Game

**Common Names:** 3 Chairs; Character Chairs  
**Observed In:** Practice / Shows

**Purpose**

**Rules**

- The three offstage players watch closely.
- When the offstage players see a button, they ring a bell.
- The seated players exit.
- The three offstage players replace them in the chairs.
- Each incoming player plays the exact same character associated with the chair they take over.
- The characters remain tied to the chairs throughout the game.
- The game continues through multiple rounds.
- The game often continues until players eventually return to their original chairs/characters.

**Character-Chair Rule**
- The chair owns the character.
- When a player sits in a chair, they inherit that chair’s character.
- The player should preserve the character’s:
  - physicality,
  - voice,
  - emotional state,
  - posture,
  - rhythm,
  - point of view,
  - relationship dynamics,
  - and repeated behaviors.
- The new player may heighten or clarify the character, but should not replace it with a different character.

**Skills Developed**
- Big character commitment.
- Physical specificity.
- Vocal specificity.
- Character inheritance.
- Observation.
- Memory.
- Repetition and heightening.
- Ensemble support.
- Button recognition.
- Editing.
- Stage picture.
- Playing inherited offers without flattening them.

**Common Failure Modes**
- Players make character choices that are too small to inherit.
- Incoming players copy only the surface voice or catchphrase and miss the body.
- Characters drift over multiple rotations until they become unrecognizable.
- Players change the character instead of heightening the existing offer.
- Offstage players miss good buttons or ring too late.
- The game becomes chaotic without clear character preservation.
- Players focus on being weird instead of being specific.
- The location disappears after the opening.
- Practice versions can become frustrating if players are pushed toward extreme absurdity before they understand the inheritance mechanic.
- Players may confuse “big character” with random Looney Tunes behavior.

**Coaching Notes**
- Big does not mean random.
- The best characters are physically large, vocally clear, and easy for another player to reproduce.
- Players should make choices that are:
  - obvious enough to copy,
  - specific enough to preserve,
  - and playable enough to sustain.
- Offstage players should watch the whole body, not just the voice.
- Incoming players should honor the existing character before adding new heightening.
- A strong replacement performance says: “I understood the offer, and now I am carrying it forward.”
- The bell should land on a button, not merely on confusion or impatience.
- Repetition is part of the game; the audience enjoys seeing the same character filtered through different performers.
- The location should continue to matter. It gives the characters something to do besides display quirks.
- The game works best when the inherited characters have clear wants, emotions, and relationships, not just physical abnormalities.

- Three players sit and establish characters.

### Foreign Film
**Type:** Short Form Game  
**Standard:** Yes  
**Common Names:** Foreign Film; Foreign Movie; Foreign Film Dub / Subtitles  
**Observed In:** Shows / Short Form Sets / Practice

**Purpose**
- Practice gibberish performance.
- Practice translating physical and vocal offers into clear meaning.
- Build scene work through tone, gesture, rhythm, and emotional intent rather than literal language.
- Train players to support partners by assigning meaning to unclear offers.
- Create comedy from the gap between what the actors appear to say and what the hosts claim they said.
- Develop hosting, framing, and audience-facing explanation skills.

**Rules**
- Four players participate.
- Two players are the hosts introducing a foreign film.
- Two players are the actors inside the film.
- The hosts ask the audience for a suggestion.
- The hosts use the suggestion to create the title of the foreign film.
- The premise is that the film is in an obscure language, but the subtitles are not working.
- The actors perform a scene speaking gibberish.
- After each gibberish line, the actor pauses.
- One of the hosts translates the line into English.
- The actors continue the scene based on the emotional and physical meaning being created by the translations.
- The game continues until the scene reaches a strong ending or the host/editor ends it.

**Actor Role**
- Speak in committed gibberish.
- Use clear emotional tone.
- Use strong physicality, gesture, posture, and facial expression.
- Pause after each gibberish line so the host can translate.
- Listen to the translation and let it affect the next gibberish line.
- Continue the scene as if the translated meaning is true.

**Host / Translator Role**
- Introduce the film clearly.
- Build a title from the audience suggestion.
- Translate each gibberish line after the actor pauses.
- Interpret the actor’s tone, physicality, gesture, and relationship energy.
- Add meaning that advances the scene.
- Clarify who, what, where, relationship, stakes, and game.
- Support the actors by giving them something playable.
- Heighten the story without overwhelming the actors.
- Preserve the illusion that the translation came from the actor’s line.

**Host Rescue / Gift Technique**
- If the actors do not provide enough gibberish, the host can temporarily shift into film-commentary mode.
- The host may explain the director’s intent, describe the artistic choice, or translate the actors’ silence, posture, facial expression, or stillness.
- This can rescue dead air without openly blaming the actors.
- Strong host rescues should also give the actors something playable.
- The host can gift an immediate physical event, emotional reaction, relationship turn, danger, discovery, or environmental change.
- Example: “The director chose silence for this moment, because in a matter of seconds they will be overcome by ocean waves crashing in, which will leave them wet and rattled.”
- This kind of rescue gives the actors a clear physical offer and a changed emotional state.
- The host should eventually steer the game back toward actor dialogue if possible.

**Skills Developed**
- Gibberish.
- Physical storytelling.
- Vocal commitment.
- Emotional readability.
- Listening.
- Justification.
- Translation of nonverbal offers.
- Hosting.
- Scene framing.
- Character support.
- Fast meaning-making.
- Audience-facing clarity.

**Common Failure Modes**
- Actors do not say enough gibberish for the hosts to translate.
- Actors mumble or undercommit vocally.
- Actors use gibberish with no emotional or physical intent.
- Actors forget to pause for translation.
- Hosts translate too literally or too cautiously.
- Hosts ignore the actor’s physical and emotional offers.
- Hosts over-control the scene and leave the actors nothing to play.
- Hosts make translations that contradict the actor’s obvious emotional tone.
- The scene becomes a series of jokes instead of a developing scene.
- Actors fail to incorporate the host’s translation into their next line or behavior.

**Coaching Notes**
- Gibberish still needs intention.
- Strong gibberish uses rhythm, volume, pauses, emotional tone, repeated sounds, gesture, eye contact, and physical relationship.
- The host should translate the whole offer, not just the sound.
- Good translations come from what the actor gives: fear becomes confession, pride becomes boast, desperation becomes plea, anger becomes accusation, tenderness becomes vulnerability.
- Hosts should translate quickly enough to preserve rhythm.
- The best translations give the actors a next playable move.
- If an actor gives too little gibberish, the host may still translate from body language, facial expression, or silence.
- The hosts should make the actors look intentional, not expose that they are struggling.

**Flying Pig Specific Notes**
- Flying Pig runs this as a four-player short-form game.
- Two players serve as hosts introducing the foreign film.
- Two players serve as actors in the film.
- The hosts get an audience gift and craft the film title from it.
- The premise is that the film is in an obscure language and the subtitles are not working, so the hosts must translate.

**Purpose**
- Practice gibberish.
- Train players to preserve meaning, emotion, and intention across language changes.
- Build confidence speaking without literal words.
- Develop physical and vocal clarity.
- Practice fast adjustment under an external cue.
- Create comedy from abrupt shifts between nonsense language and English.
- Train players to continue the same scene rather than restarting every time the language changes.

**Rules**
- Two players perform a scene.
- A third player stands offstage or to the side with a bell.
- The scene begins in gibberish.
- Whenever the bell rings, the players immediately switch languages.
- If they were speaking gibberish, they switch to English.
- If they were speaking English, they switch back to gibberish.
- The bell may ring during a sentence, forcing the player to continue the same thought in the new language.
- The scene continues until it reaches a strong ending or the host/editor ends it.

**Bell Player Role**
- Control the timing of language switches.
- Ring the bell often enough to keep the game active.
- Avoid ringing so often that the scene becomes impossible to follow.
- Use the bell to create pressure, surprise, and rhythm.
- Ringing in the middle of a sentence is especially useful because it forces the player to preserve the thought across the switch.

**Scene Player Role**
- Start in committed gibberish.
- Maintain clear emotional intent even when speaking nonsense.
- When the bell rings, immediately switch language.
- Continue the same sentence, thought, emotion, or action after the switch.
- Do not treat the switch as a reset.
- Use physicality, tone, gesture, and relationship to make the gibberish readable.
- Let the English portions clarify what the gibberish portions implied.

**Common Failure Modes**
- Players freeze when the bell rings.
- Players restart the sentence instead of continuing the same thought.
- The gibberish has no emotional or physical intention.
- Players use gibberish as filler instead of communication.
- English sections become exposition dumps.
- Players wait for the bell instead of playing the scene.
- The bell player rings too often and prevents development.
- The bell player waits too long and the game mechanic disappears.

**Coaching Notes**
- The strongest version preserves the sentence across the switch.
- If the bell rings mid-sentence, continue the same thought in the new language.
- Gibberish should still have syntax, rhythm, emotional tone, and intention.
- The audience does not need to understand every gibberish word; they need to understand the emotional and relational direction.
- Good gibberish has shape. It should sound like a real language even though it is not one.
- Strong physical choices make the gibberish much easier for the audience to follow.

**Difference from Foreign Film**
Foreign Film uses gibberish actors and host-translators. The actors pause after each gibberish line so the hosts can translate and assign meaning.

Gibberish Switch has no translators. The two scene players themselves move back and forth between gibberish and English. The challenge is preserving scene continuity through the switches.

**Flying Pig Specific Notes**
- Flying Pig runs this as a two-player scene with a third player controlling a bell.
- The players start in gibberish.
- Each bell ring switches the scene from gibberish to English or from English back to gibberish.
- The bell often rings in the middle of a sentence.
- Gibberish Switch is personally notable for Troy.
- The first time Troy practiced this game, he failed badly.
- The most recent time Troy practiced it, he felt good about his performance.
- Troy is still not the strongest player at the game because his gibberish work has more room to grow.
- However, this may be Troy’s most improved individual short-form game.
- The improvement appears to come from better comfort with gibberish, stronger willingness to commit vocally, and improved ability to preserve the same thought across the switch between gibberish and English.

### New Choice
**Type:** Short Form Game  
**Standard:** Yes  
**Common Names:** New Choice; Ding; Should’ve Said  
**Observed In:** Shows / Short Form Sets / Practice / Clockwork Group  
**Observed Frequency:** Often

**Purpose**
- Train players to make fast alternate offers.
- Break players out of predictable first instincts.
- Practice flexibility under pressure.
- Encourage bigger, clearer, stranger, or more playable choices.
- Create comedy from rapid reversals, escalations, and substitutions.
- Develop the ability to abandon a safe offer and immediately commit to a new one.

**Rules**
- Two players perform a scene.
- A third player serves as the caller.
- At any point, the caller may say “New Choice.”
- The player who just spoke or acted must immediately replace their previous offer with a new one.
- The new choice becomes the reality of the scene.
- The scene continues from the new choice.
- The caller may call “New Choice” multiple times in a row.
- The caller may target dialogue, physical action, emotional reaction, object work, relationship offers, or character behavior.

**Caller Role**
- Watch the scene actively.
- Call “New Choice” often enough that the game stays alive.
- Use the call to push players away from safe, obvious, vague, or low-energy offers.
- Call again if the replacement choice is only a synonym or minor wording change.
- Help the scene find stronger meaning, clearer stakes, bigger emotion, or a more playable direction.
- Avoid calling so constantly that the scene cannot develop.

**Scene Player Role**
- Immediately replace the previous offer when “New Choice” is called.
- Do not explain or resist the call.
- Treat the newest choice as true.
- Make the replacement meaningfully different.
- Commit fully to the new reality, even if it surprises you.
- Let the new choice affect the relationship, stakes, behavior, or world.
- Avoid giving only a new word with the same meaning.

**Good New Choices**
A strong new choice changes the scene in a meaningful way.

Example:
- Original: “I brought you flowers.”
- Weak new choice: “I brought you roses.”
- Strong new choice: “I brought you divorce papers.”
- Stronger new choice: “I brought you the raccoon that ruined our wedding.”

The best replacements often change intention, relationship, status, emotion, danger, specificity, object, location, history, or the game of the scene.

**Common Failure Modes**
- The caller does not call “New Choice” often enough.
- The caller waits too long and the game mechanic disappears.
- Players replace a word but preserve the same meaning.
- Players make only tiny cosmetic changes.
- Players hesitate while searching for a clever alternative.
- Players treat the new choice as a joke instead of the new truth.
- Players resist or explain the rejected choice.
- The caller calls too often and prevents the scene from developing.
- The new choices become random instead of playable.

**Coaching Notes**
- A new choice should usually be a new meaning, not a synonym.
- Fast is good, but fast plus meaningful is better.
- If the first replacement is weak, the caller should call “New Choice” again.
- Players should not try to protect the original idea.
- The audience enjoys watching the performer abandon safety and discover something better.
- The best versions keep the relationship alive while the details mutate.
- Teaching phrase: “Don’t give me a new word. Give me a new world.”

**Flying Pig Specific Notes**
- Flying Pig and the Clockwork group play New Choice often.
- Troy is pretty good at this game.
- Troy enjoys playing it and loves watching strong players perform it.
- In practice and in the Clockwork group, the game often suffers because the caller does not call “New Choice” often enough.
- The caller role is important; under-calling makes the game feel like a normal scene with occasional interruptions rather than a true New Choice game.
- Bree and Derek can struggle as scene players because they often respond quickly but replace the line with a new word or phrase that has the same basic meaning.
- Their speed is useful, but the game works best when the new choice changes the meaning, not just the wording.
- Troy’s useful distinction: the replacement choice should create a new playable reality, not merely a synonym.

### Entrances and Exits
**Type:** Short Form Game  
**Standard:** Yes  
**Common Names:** Entrances and Exits; Key Word; Enter Exit  
**Observed In:** Shows / Short Form Sets / Practice  
**Player Count:** Usually 3 players, but can vary

**Purpose**
- Practice listening for cue words.
- Train justified entrances and exits.
- Build scene flexibility under a mechanical constraint.
- Create comedy from forced appearances and disappearances.
- Encourage players to make entrances and exits matter inside the scene.
- Develop awareness of who is currently onstage and who is offstage.

**Rules**
- Each player is assigned a common word from the audience.
- The players begin a scene.
- Whenever a player’s assigned word is spoken, that player must change stage status:
  - if they are offstage, they enter;
  - if they are onstage, they exit.
- Players must justify entrances and exits within the reality of the scene.
- The scene continues until it reaches a strong ending or the host/editor ends it.

**Common Failure Modes**
- Players forget their cue word.
- Players miss their entrance or exit.
- Players enter or exit without justification.
- The scene becomes only about saying cue words.
- Players avoid cue words so the mechanic disappears.
- Players overuse cue words and create chaos.
- Common words are chosen that are too common or too rare.
- Entrances and exits become mechanical instead of playable.
- Players stop developing relationship, stakes, or location.

**Coaching Notes**
- Choose words that are common enough to appear but not so common that the scene becomes impossible.
- The entrance or exit should feel motivated, not arbitrary.
- A forced exit can become a gift: sudden embarrassment, urgent phone call, fear, betrayal, allergic reaction, obligation, dramatic exit, or emotional overwhelm.
- A forced entrance should bring energy, information, or pressure.
- Do not just walk on or off. Make the movement part of the story.
- The scene still needs who, what, where, and relationship.

**Flying Pig Specific Notes**
- Troy has seen Entrances and Exits performed onstage.
- Troy has not personally practiced this game.
- The game usually involves about three players in the Flying Pig version.
- Each player receives a common audience-suggested word.
- Whenever a player’s assigned word is spoken, that player must enter if offstage or exit if onstage.
- The main challenge is listening for the cue word while still justifying entrances and exits as part of a real scene.

**Post-V4 Reconciliation Additions**
- Usually three players with trigger words.
- Often begins with a two-player scene.
- Multiple triggers can fire simultaneously.
- Entrances and exits should be justified.
- Common failures:
  - Missing trigger words.
  - Accidentally saying your own trigger word.

### Number of Words
**Type:** Short Form Game  
**Standard:** Yes  
**Common Names:** Number of Words; Word Count; Fixed Number of Words  
**Observed In:** Shows / Short Form Sets / Practice  
**Player Count:** Usually 3 players

**Purpose**
- Practice scene work under a verbal constraint.
- Train players to speak clearly and economically.
- Build awareness of sentence structure.
- Encourage players to make simple, playable offers.
- Create comedy from uneven speaking limits.
- Force players to listen and respond while managing a technical rule.

**Rules**
- Three players perform a scene.
- Each player is assigned a number, usually between 1 and 7.
- The assigned number is the exact number of words that player must use in every line of dialogue.
- The players perform a normal scene while obeying their word-count restrictions.
- Each spoken line must contain exactly the assigned number of words.
- The scene continues until it reaches a strong ending or the host/editor ends it.

**Example**
- Player A: 1 word per line.
- Player B: 4 words per line.
- Player C: 7 words per line.

Player A: “Dad.”  
Player B: “You missed dinner again.”  
Player C: “I was trapped inside the old carnival.”

**Common Failure Modes**
- Players lose count.
- Players pause too long while counting words.
- Players focus so much on the number that the scene becomes flat.
- Players use filler words to hit the count.
- Players abandon relationship, stakes, or location.
- The one-word player becomes too passive.
- The high-word-count player rambles without adding meaning.
- Players correct themselves out loud and break the scene.

**Coaching Notes**
- The word count is the constraint, not the scene.
- Simple sentences are usually better than clever ones.
- The player with fewer words should use strong, loaded words.
- The player with more words should avoid padding and use the extra words to add specificity.
- If a player makes a counting mistake, recover and keep moving unless the format requires correction.
- Physical action can carry meaning when the verbal constraint is tight.

**Flying Pig Specific Notes**
- Troy remembers this game as “Number of Words” or something similar.
- The Flying Pig version typically uses three players.
- Each player is assigned a number of words, usually between 1 and 7.
- Every line spoken by each player must contain exactly that player’s assigned number of words.
- The game rewards players who can manage a technical verbal constraint without losing the scene.

**Post-V4 Reconciliation Additions**
- Usually three players.
- Sometimes four.
- Audience assigns numbers 1–7.
- Contractions are allowed.
- Players are expected to count.
- Incorrect counts are buzzed.
- Success largely comes from practice.

### World’s Worst
**Type:** Short Form Game  
**Standard:** Yes  
**Common Names:** World’s Worst; Worst Of  
**Observed In:** Shows / Short Form Sets  
**Observed Frequency:** Seen once performed by the troupe

**Purpose**
- Practice rapid joke generation.
- Build comfort stepping forward with imperfect ideas.
- Train concise premise-to-punchline execution.
- Encourage strong character snapshots.
- Create a fast-paced run of bad examples from an audience suggestion.
- Reward confidence, brevity, and commitment.

**Rules**
- Several players stand in a line or group.
- The host asks the audience for a suggestion.
- The suggestion is usually a profession, role, situation, object, or category.
- The host announces the prompt as “World’s worst [suggestion].”
- Players step forward one at a time.
- Each player delivers a short line, physical bit, character moment, or example of the world’s worst version of that thing.
- After delivering the bit, the player steps back.
- Other players continue stepping forward with new examples.
- The host ends the round when the energy dips or a strong button lands.
- The host may then get a new suggestion and start another round.

**Examples**
- World’s worst doctor: “Good news. I watched half a YouTube video.”
- World’s worst wedding officiant: “Do either of you have any final objections to me personally?”
- World’s worst babysitter: “Whoever finds the snake first gets dessert.”
- World’s worst firefighter: “Before I begin, is this a rental?”

**Common Failure Modes**
- Players wait too long for a perfect joke.
- A small number of players carry the whole round.
- Bits run too long.
- Players explain the joke.
- Multiple players use the same angle.
- The game becomes too mean, gross, or obvious.
- Players step forward without a clear premise.
- The host lets a weak round continue too long.

**Coaching Notes**
- Speed matters more than perfection.
- One clear joke is better than a long setup.
- Step forward on a good-enough idea.
- Commit physically and vocally.
- Get out cleanly after the joke lands.
- The host should end the round on a strong laugh or when energy begins to drop.
- The best examples are short, specific, and instantly understandable.
- Weak jokes are survivable if the pace stays high.
- Hesitation hurts the game more than a mediocre line.

- Typically five performers.
- Host gathers categories.
- Players step forward one at a time.
- Strong examples creatively demonstrate incompetence.

### Luxury / Luxury!
**Type:** Short Form Game  
**Standard:** Common short-form game / Flying Pig observed  
**Common Names:** Luxury; Luxury!  
**Observed In:** Flying Pig show  
**Observed Frequency:** Troy saw it in a February Flying Pig show  
**Status:** Reviewed / Troy-confirmed

**Purpose**
- Practice reframing.
- Practice escalation through contrast.
- Build strong character point of view.
- Turn complaints into status play.
- Train players to heighten by making the previous problem seem easy compared to their own.
- Create a repeatable comic structure based on interruption, reversal, and topping.

**Setup**
- Four players are onstage.
- The host gets a pet peeve, minor complaint, annoyance, or everyday problem from the audience.
- One player begins by ranting about how much they hate that pet peeve or problem.
- The other players listen for an opportunity to interrupt.

**Sequence of Play**
1. Player 1 begins ranting about the audience-provided pet peeve or complaint.
2. Another player interrupts by yelling: "Luxury!"
3. The interrupting player explains why Player 1's complaint is actually easy, privileged, or desirable compared to their own situation.
4. The interrupting player then begins ranting about their own worse problem.
5. Another player interrupts that rant by yelling: "Luxury!"
6. That next player reframes the previous complaint as comparatively fortunate and introduces an even worse or more absurd problem.
7. The pattern continues: complaint, "Luxury!", reframe, worse complaint.
8. Players keep the characters they create throughout the game.
9. The game continues until the host or performers find a strong final button or the escalation has peaked.

**Core Game Pattern**
- "You think that's bad? That would be a luxury compared to my situation."
- Each new player should both justify why the previous problem is not actually so bad, and introduce a worse, more specific, more playable problem.

**Important Rules**
- The interrupting word is "Luxury!"
- The previous complaint must be reframed as preferable, not simply ignored.
- The new complaint should heighten or shift the scale of misery, inconvenience, status, absurdity, or specificity.
- Players should keep the characters they establish.
- The game is not just a list of bad things; it is a chain of comparative suffering and comic reframing.

**Skills Developed**
- Reframing.
- Escalation.
- Character point of view.
- Status contrast.
- Justification.
- Listening for a clean interruption point.
- Concise ranting.
- Heightening through specificity.
- Callback potential.
- Playing a repeatable short-form structure.

**Common Failure Modes**
- Players yell "Luxury!" but do not clearly explain why the previous problem is preferable.
- New complaints are not worse, sharper, or more specific.
- Players abandon their characters after their first rant.
- The game becomes a generic complaint list instead of a comparative escalation game.
- Players interrupt too quickly before the prior complaint is clear.
- Players wait too long and the rant loses energy.
- Complaints become too abstract to play.
- Players top only through darkness or extremity instead of through specificity, status, absurdity, or character logic.
- The game lacks a final button and trails off.

**Coaching Notes**
- The pleasure of the game is in the reversal: the thing one person hates becomes another person's dream.
- The "Luxury!" interruption should be confident and theatrical.
- The reframe matters as much as the new complaint.
- Specific misery beats generic misery.
- Players should avoid merely escalating to trauma or disaster unless the tone of the show supports it.
- Strong versions use character logic: each player's worse problem reveals who they are.
- The best complaints are playable, specific, and easy for the audience to understand quickly.
- Keep the rhythm moving: rant, interrupt, reframe, heighten.

**What Success Looks Like**
- The audience understands each reframe immediately.
- Each "Luxury!" gets a laugh because it flips the prior complaint.
- The problems escalate or become more absurd in a clear pattern.
- Players maintain distinct characters.
- The game builds momentum instead of becoming repetitive.
- The final beat feels like the chain has reached a comic peak.

**Flying Pig Specific Notes**
- Troy saw this performed at Flying Pig in a February show.
- The game was introduced as "Luxury."
- Troy initially remembered the name but not the structure.
- After being reminded of the standard short-form version, Troy confirmed this was definitely the game.
- Cataloged as Luxury / Luxury! so it can be found by either name.


### Survivor
**Type:** Short Form Game  
**Standard:** Standard/common improv replay-elimination structure / Flying Pig observed  
**Common Names:** Survivor; Shrinking Scene  
**Observed In:** Shows / Short Form Sets / Practice  
**Player Count:** 5 scene players plus a host

**Purpose**
- Practice scene memory.
- Practice replaying and preserving a shared scene structure.
- Train ensemble support as players disappear from the scene.
- Build physical and vocal clarity across multiple characters.
- Create comedy from increasing burden as fewer players cover more roles.
- Reward listening, recall, and character distinctiveness.

**Setup**
- One host runs the game.
- Five players begin a scene together.
- The scene should establish clear who, what, and where.
- Each scene player should have at least one line during the initial run.
- The initial version should create enough memorable lines, actions, relationships, and stage positions to be replayed.

**Sequence of Play**
1. The five scene players perform the full scene.
2. After the first run, the host selects one player to remove.
3. The remaining four players replay the scene from the beginning.
4. The missing player’s lines, actions, and contributions are covered by the remaining players.
5. Coverage is flexible: any remaining player may jump in, and multiple players may share the removed player’s contributions.
6. After each replay, the host removes another player.
7. The scene resets from the beginning each time.
8. The process continues until only one player remains.
9. The final player performs the entire scene solo, covering all characters, lines, physical actions, and interactions.

**Host Responsibilities**
- Introduce and frame the game.
- Start the initial five-player scene.
- Decide which player is removed after each replay.
- Optionally ask the audience to vote on removals.
- Audience voting is especially useful when the game is down to the final two players.
- Keep the pace moving between replays.
- Preserve the elimination/replay structure clearly for the audience.
- End the game after the final solo version.

**Rules and Performance Expectations**
- The replay should be as exact as possible.
- Exactness includes key lines, broad structure, character moments, and major physical actions.
- The replay will inevitably become imperfect, and that imperfection is part of the comedy.
- Players should not intentionally abandon the established scene just because someone has been removed.
- The missing player’s material should be supported, not dropped.
- The final solo player should distinguish characters through physicality, voice, posture, location, and rhythm.

**Skills Developed**
- Listening.
- Memory.
- Ensemble support.
- Character recall.
- Physical and vocal differentiation.
- Scene structure tracking.
- Replay discipline.
- Fast coverage of missing material.
- Confidence under increasing exposure.
- Solo performance clarity.

**Common Failure Modes**
- The original scene is too vague to replay.
- Not every player gets a memorable line or contribution in the initial run.
- Removed players’ lines are forgotten or dropped.
- Remaining players wait for someone else to cover the missing material.
- Multiple players try to cover the same missing beat and create confusion.
- The replay changes too much too early.
- Players focus so hard on memory that they lose performance energy.
- Character shifts are too subtle in the final solo version.
- The host lets transitions drag between replays.
- Audience voting slows the game instead of heightening suspense.

**Coaching Notes**
- The first scene needs clear landmarks.
- Every player should give the group something memorable enough to replay.
- Replays should preserve the scene’s skeleton: order of events, key lines, physical beats, and major relationships.
- As players are removed, the remaining players should cover missing material decisively.
- Do not wait for permission to cover a missing line or action.
- The final solo performance should be energetic and readable, not merely accurate.
- Physical and vocal separation between characters becomes increasingly important as the scene shrinks.
- Strong replays are consistent enough for the audience to recognize the original scene but imperfect enough to create surprise.

**Flying Pig Specific Notes**
- “Shrinking Scene” is a working name, not a confirmed official Flying Pig title.
- The version documented here uses one host and five scene players.
- The host is not part of the scene.
- The host usually picks which player is removed.
- The host may ask the audience to vote, especially when choosing between the final two players.
- The scene resets from the beginning after each removal.
- Remaining players may divide up the removed player’s lines and actions however they can.
- The game succeeds when the shrinking cast preserves the recognizable scene while making each replay more difficult, more exposed, and funnier.

**What Success Looks Like**
- The original scene has clear who, what, where, and memorable contributions from every player.
- Each replay remains recognizably the same scene.
- Missing lines and actions are covered smoothly.
- The audience enjoys seeing how the remaining players redistribute the removed roles.
- The final solo performance is clear, energetic, and entertaining.

### Growing and Shrinking
**Type:** Short Form Game / Long-Form-Adjacent Structure  
**Standard:** Yes  
**Common Names:** Growing and Shrinking; Growing and Shrinking Machine; Space Jump; Onion; Pyramids; Fir Tree  
**Observed In:** Shows / Practice / Long Form Sets  
**Player Count:** Usually 4–6; Flying Pig observed version uses 6 players

**Purpose**
- Practice scene memory.
- Practice clear scene initiations.
- Train players to create distinct scenes from the same stage picture or player group.
- Build skill in returning to earlier scenes after interruptions.
- Develop ensemble awareness, physical recall, and structural discipline.
- Create comedy from contrast between unrelated scenes and the eventual return through earlier layers.

**Core Structure**
Growing and Shrinking begins with a small scene and grows one player at a time until all players are onstage. Then it shrinks back down as players leave in reverse order, returning to earlier scenes.

A simple six-player structure:
- Player A begins alone, often with a monologue or solo scene.
- Player B enters and begins a new scene with Player A.
- Player C enters and begins a completely new scene with Players A and B.
- Player D enters and begins a completely new scene with Players A, B, and C.
- Player E enters and begins a completely new scene with Players A, B, C, and D.
- Player F enters and begins a completely new scene with all six players.

Then the structure reverses:
- Player F exits, and the group returns to the previous five-person scene.
- Player E exits, and the group returns to the previous four-person scene.
- Player D exits, and the group returns to the previous three-person scene.
- Player C exits, and the group returns to the previous two-person scene.
- Player B exits, and Player A returns to the original solo scene or monologue.

**Important Rule**
Each new entrance creates a whole new scene unrelated to the previous one.

Each exit returns the remaining players to the scene they were doing before that player entered.

**Common Failure Modes**
- Players forget the earlier scenes.
- Players resume the wrong scene after an exit.
- New scenes are not distinct enough from prior scenes.
- Players fail to create a clear new reality when entering.
- Players exit in the wrong order.
- The group forgets physical positions or relationships.
- Players over-focus on memory and stop playing the scene.
- The first few scenes are too vague to remember later.
- The full-stage scene becomes chaotic and hard to unwind.

**Coaching Notes**
- Each scene needs a strong, memorable label in the players’ minds.
- Make early scenes simple and specific.
- The first line of each new scene should strongly establish the new reality.
- Use different relationships, locations, body positions, and emotional tones for each layer.
- Physical specificity helps memory.
- When returning to an earlier scene, restart with the strongest remembered offer.
- The shrinking half is the payoff, so the growing half must establish clean, memorable scenes.

**Flying Pig Specific Notes**
- Troy remembers this as “Growing and Shrinking.”
- The observed Flying Pig version uses six players.
- A player opens with a monologue or solo scene.
- Player 2 joins and creates a whole new scene unrelated to the monologue.
- Player 3 enters and creates another whole new scene.
- This continues until all six players are onstage.
- Then players leave the stage, and the group returns to earlier scenes in reverse order.
- The game requires memory and structural tracking.
- Troy likes watching this game.
- Troy likes practicing it, but has not gotten many reps.
- This form likely fits Troy’s structural strengths, but it also pressures memory and clean scene labeling.

## Long Form Structures and Techniques

### Montage
**Type:** Long Form Structure  
**Standard:** Yes  
**Common Names:** Montage; Monologue Montage; Armando-style Montage Opening  
**House Variant:** Flying Pig monologue chain into two-person scene series  
**Observed In:** Advanced Class / Shows / Long Form Sets

**Purpose**
- Generate grounded scene inspiration from truthful personal material.
- Practice long-form editing.
- Build scenes from theme, emotion, relationship, image, or premise rather than literal suggestion.
- Develop ensemble awareness across a longer improvised set.
- Encourage players to initiate clearly and edit generously.

**Rules**
- All players participate.
- The audience is asked for a suggestion.
- One player steps forward and gives a true monologue inspired by the audience suggestion.
- A second player steps forward and gives a true monologue inspired by the first player’s monologue.
- A third player steps forward and gives a true monologue inspired by the second player’s monologue.
- After the three monologues, the troupe repeats the original audience suggestion and claps.
- A series of improvised scenes begins.
- Scenes are inspired by one of the monologues, the original suggestion, or ideas/themes/images generated by the opening.
- Scenes typically begin as two-person scenes.
- Scenes may grow beyond two players if the scene calls for it.
- Any off-scene player may edit a scene at any time.
- Edits should happen when the scene reaches a strong moment or begins to stall.
- After an edit, the next scene should generally begin with two players.

**Skills Developed**
- Truthful monologue work.
- Thematic listening.
- Scenic initiation.
- Long-form patience.
- Editing.
- Pattern recognition.
- Starting grounded two-person scenes.
- Drawing inspiration without copying literally.
- Ensemble pacing.
- Knowing when to enter, support, heighten, or cut.

**Common Failure Modes**
- Monologues become too long or too performative.
- Players pull only literal details from the monologues instead of deeper themes or emotional material.
- Scenes become disconnected in a way that feels random rather than inspired.
- Players wait too long to edit scenes after they have peaked.
- Players edit too early before the relationship or game has formed.
- Scenes start with vague premise instead of clear who/what/where.

### Town Hall
**Type:** Long Form Structure / Show Opener  
**Standard:** Common Long-Form Premise Structure / Possible House Variant  
**Observed In:** Shows  
**Common Names:** Town Hall; HOA Meeting; City Council Meeting  
**House Variant:** Flying Pig audience-planted character introduction into two-person scene montage

**Purpose**
- Start a show with immediate audience interaction.
- Establish characters publicly before scene work begins.
- Create a shared fictional community or civic problem.
- Generate character POVs from a single audience suggestion.
- Transition from direct-address character introductions into long-form scene work.
- Let the audience discover that some “audience members” are actually performers.

**Rules**
- The form begins as a public meeting, usually framed as an HOA meeting, town hall, or city council meeting.
- One player is not undercover and serves as the meeting leader or facilitator.
- The other players are planted undercover in the audience.
- The meeting leader brings the meeting to order.
- The meeting leader asks the real audience for a suggestion.
- The meeting leader begins calling on the planted players in the audience.
- Each planted player responds in character, giving their take on the issue and introducing themselves.
- Once all player-characters have been introduced, the performers move to the stage.
- The long-form scene sequence begins.
- Scenes are performed in the characters established during the town hall opening.
- Scenes usually begin as two-person scenes.
- Off-scene players may edit scenes when they choose.
- When the form reaches time, a strong culmination, or a natural endpoint, someone calls the end of the game.
- After the long-form ends, someone introduces or transitions into the rest of the show.

**Skills Developed**
- Character introduction.
- Public-address character work.
- Strong point of view.
- Audience interaction.
- Listening for usable details.
- Ensemble world-building.
- Transitioning from premise/opening into scenes.
- Long-form editing.
- Reusing established character information.
- Building scenes from civic conflict, local stakes, and community relationships.

**Common Failure Modes**
- Undercover players do not commit strongly enough before the reveal.
- Character introductions become too similar to each other.
- Players give jokes instead of playable characters.
- The opening generates opinions but not relationships.
- Players move to stage without carrying forward the details established in the town hall.
- The later scenes ignore the original issue or community context.
- The form becomes a sequence of speeches instead of scenes.
- The meeting leader calls on players too slowly, causing the opening to drag.
- Players over-explain the civic premise instead of making clear character offers.
- The audience may become confused if the undercover-player reveal is not clear enough.

**Coaching Notes**
- Each planted player should enter with a clear character POV, not just a funny complaint.
- The town hall portion should generate usable scenic material:
  - relationships,
  - grudges,
  - status differences,
  - local history,
  - rival factions,
  - personal stakes,
  - specific locations,
  - repeated community problems.
- The meeting leader should keep the opening moving and make sure every player is introduced.
- Once onstage, players should honor the characters and offers established during the meeting.
- Scenes should not merely restate the meeting opinions; they should dramatize the relationships underneath them.
- The best scenes usually come from asking:
  - Who already knows each other?
  - Who is hiding something?
  - Who benefits from the town issue?
  - Who is emotionally overinvested?
  - Who has status in this community?
- Edits should happen when a scene peaks or begins to stall, consistent with Flying Pig’s broader long-form editing notes.

**Flying Pig Specific Notes**
- Flying Pig sometimes uses Town Hall to start shows.
- The premise is usually an HOA meeting, city council meeting, or similar public civic meeting.
- One player visibly leads the meeting.
- The rest of the players begin undercover in the audience.
- The leader asks for an audience get, then calls on the undercover players for their opinions and introductions.
- After all performers have been introduced, the players move from the audience to the stage.
- The stage portion consists of scenes using the characters established during the meeting.
- Scenes typically start as two-person scenes but may expand if useful.
- Off-scene players edit when they desire.
- When the long-form reaches time or culmination, someone ends the game and introduces the show.
- This structure aligns with Flying Pig’s preference for clear character setup, strong audience-facing framing, and long-form scenes that begin from established offers rather than abstract discovery.

### Repeater
**Type:** Long Form Technique / Transition Structure  
**Standard:** Unknown; may be known under another name elsewhere  
**Common Names:** Repeater  
**Operating Name:** Repeater  
**Observed In:** Advanced Class / Long Form Practice  
**Flying Pig Status:** Jesse has decided that “Repeater” is the operating name for this technique

**Purpose**
- Practice self-editing from inside a scene.
- Practice transforming a line through character, voice, physicality, and context.
- Create seamless long-form transitions.
- Train players to recognize when a scene has reached an exit point.
- Encourage players to initiate new scenes from existing material.
- Build a chain of scenes connected by repeated dialogue rather than shared plot.

**Rules**
- Two players begin a scene.
- At some point, one player repeats back the last line spoken by the other player.
- The repeated line is delivered as a new character, with a clear change in physicality, voice, emotional state, or context.
- The player whose line was repeated exits the stage.
- A new player enters and responds to the repeated line as the beginning of a new scene.
- The new scene continues until another player repeats the last line in a new character and triggers the next transition.
- The form continues as a chain of scenes, each launched by a repeated line transformed into a new context.

**Transition Mechanics**
- The repeated line functions as the edit.
- The line should be repeated clearly enough that the audience and players recognize the transition.
- The repeating player must make the new character/context unmistakable.
- The exiting player should leave promptly once the edit is clear.
- A new player should enter quickly to support the newly established character and scene.
- The transition works best when the repeated line feels like both:
  - a callback to the previous scene,
  - and a playable first line of a new scene.

**Skills Developed**
- Self-editing.
- Scenic awareness.
- Character transformation.
- Physical and vocal differentiation.
- Listening for strong final lines.
- Initiating from existing text.
- Ensemble responsiveness.
- Recognizing scene stalls.
- Transition clarity.
- Long-form pacing.
- Commitment to new character and context.

**Common Failure Modes**
- Players repeat a line during a normal scene without intending an edit.
- Players slightly change the repeated line, creating uncertainty about whether an edit is happening.
- The repeated line is not delivered with a clear enough new character or physical shift.
- The exiting player does not realize they are supposed to leave.
- Offstage players hesitate and fail to enter quickly.
- The group treats the repeated line as dialogue instead of a transition.
- Players wait for someone else to edit instead of self-editing.
- Weaker scene players stall but do not recognize that they should use the technique to escape and transform the scene.
- The technique produces a chained failure condition: the players most in need of the edit are often the least able to identify and execute it.
- The form becomes confusing if the ensemble lacks shared agreement about what counts as a transition.

**Coaching Notes**
- The edit must be unmistakable.
- The repeated line should be as exact as possible.
- A clear physical and vocal shift is required; subtlety will confuse the ensemble.
- The repeating player should not merely echo the line. They should transform it into a new character’s first line.
- The exiting player should trust the edit and leave cleanly.
- The entering player should join quickly and define the new reality.
- This technique requires players to be comfortable editing themselves, not just being edited by others.
- The group should practice the transition mechanics separately before expecting full scenes to support the form.
- A useful teaching progression may be:
  - practice repeating exact lines with large character shifts,
  - practice exit/entrance timing without full scenes,
  - run short two-line transitions,
  - then build into full chained scenes.
- The audience needs to understand the pattern. Early transitions should be especially clear.

**Difference from 3 Rooms (formerly French Braid)**
Repeater uses a repeated line from inside the active scene as the edit into a new scene. The player who repeats the line transforms into a new character or context, and the player whose line was repeated exits.

3 Rooms also uses repeated lines in the Flying Pig version, but the repeated line works as a cross-pair trigger. The pair repeating the line triggers another pair’s scene; it does not trigger its own scene.

**Flying Pig Specific Notes**
- Jesse has decided that “Repeater” is the operating name for this technique.
- The entry should no longer treat Repeater as merely a temporary placeholder, even though the form may have another standard name elsewhere.
- Troy loves this technique and thinks it could become his favorite long-form technique if the group could handle the transitions cleanly.
- The advanced class has struggled to learn it.
- A major difficulty is that many advanced-class players are not comfortable editing themselves from inside a scene.
- Troy is a clear exception and is comfortable self-editing.
- Jesse herself appears less comfortable with self-editing in this specific structure.
- Steve did fairly well after initially failing badly on one cycle.
- Bree figured it out.
- Kim and Derek have created confusion by repeating a line during normal scene dialogue, often with slight changes, making it unclear whether they intended an edit.
- The technique is especially vulnerable to unclear transition signals.
- The form’s biggest structural problem is that stalled players often do not think to edit themselves, because the players most likely to stall are also the players with weaker scene-structure awareness.
- The technique rewards players who can identify a scene’s turning point, make a bold character shift, and trust the ensemble to follow the edit.

**What Success Looks Like**
- The repeated line is exact enough to be recognized.
- The physical/vocal/context shift is unmistakable.
- The old scene exits cleanly.
- The new scene begins immediately.
- The transition feels like discovery rather than confusion.
- The chain maintains momentum across multiple scenes.

### La Ronde
**Type:** Long Form Structure  
**Standard:** Yes  
**Common Names:** La Ronde; The Round; Character Chain  
**Observed In:** Shows / Long Form Sets  
**Flying Pig Variant:** Offstage tag-in version

**Purpose**
- Practice linked two-person scenes.
- Build a long-form structure through character continuity.
- Train players to preserve and explore characters across multiple relationships.
- Create clean transitions between scenes.
- Reveal different sides of the same character by placing them with different scene partners.
- Build audience-readable continuity without requiring a single plot.

**Standard Structure**
La Ronde is a chain of two-person scenes.

The basic pattern is:
- Player A and Player B perform a scene.
- Player A leaves or is tagged out.
- Player B remains onstage as the same character.
- Player C enters as a new character and begins a new scene with Player B.
- Then Player B leaves or is tagged out.
- Player C remains as the same character.
- Player D enters as a new character.
- The pattern continues until the form eventually circles back.

A simple version looks like:
- Scene 1: A + B
- Scene 2: B + C
- Scene 3: C + D
- Scene 4: D + E
- Scene 5: E + F
- Scene 6: F + A

The defining feature is that each new scene shares one continuing character with the previous scene.

**Flying Pig Tag-In Version**
In the Flying Pig version Troy has observed, the transition may be triggered by an offstage player tagging in for one of the players currently onstage.

The tag-in player replaces one player from the active two-person scene. The other player remains onstage as the same character, and the new player begins a new scene with them.

Example:
- Scene 1: A + B
- Offstage player C tags out A.
- Scene 2: B + C
- Offstage player D tags out B.
- Scene 3: C + D
- Offstage player E tags out C.
- Scene 4: D + E

This preserves the standard La Ronde structure because one character carries forward into each new scene.

**Common Failure Modes**
- The remaining player changes character instead of preserving the same one.
- The entering player does not clearly establish a new relationship.
- Scenes feel too similar because each new partner plays the same dynamic.
- Players treat the transition like a simple tag-out game rather than a character-chain form.
- The offstage player tags in before the active scene has established enough character or relationship.
- The audience loses track of which character is continuing.

**Coaching Notes**
- The continuing character is the anchor.
- Each new scene should reveal something new about the character who remained.
- The entering player should give the continuing character a new relationship immediately.
- The tag-in should happen on a strong moment, not merely when a player feels like entering.
- The new scene should not just continue the same conversation with a different person.
- Strong La Ronde play asks: “Who else knows this person, and what different side of them do they bring out?”

**Difference from Repeater**
Repeater uses a repeated line as the transition mechanism. A player inside the scene repeats the partner’s last line in a new character or context, causing a new scene to begin.

La Ronde does not require a repeated line. Its defining mechanism is character continuity: one player remains as the same character while a new player enters and begins a new scene.

**Flying Pig Specific Notes**
- Jesse recognized La Ronde as a known form and distinguished it from Repeater.
- The key distinction appears to be that La Ronde edits are triggered from offstage, while Repeater edits are triggered from inside the active scene.
- Troy has seen La Ronde performed onstage.
- Troy initially confused La Ronde with a possible repeated-line rotation structure because of its relationship to Repeater and offstage-triggered edits.
- The version Troy has seen appears to use an offstage player tagging in for one of the active players while keeping the other player present.
- This should be documented as the Flying Pig tag-in version of standard La Ronde.

### 3 Rooms
**Type:** Long Form Structure / Transition Technique  
**Standard:** Yes, with Flying Pig-specific mechanics documented here  
**Common Names:** French Braid  
**Observed In:** Shows / Long Form Sets  
**Status:** Observed multiple times; Flying Pig mechanics reconstructed from memory and later clarified

**Purpose**
- Create a braided long-form structure from multiple recurring scene threads.
- Practice interrupting and returning to scenes.
- Train players to listen from offstage and recognize strong transition lines.
- Build momentum through recurring two-person scene strands.
- Practice justifying a repeated line in a new or returning context.
- Develop ensemble timing, pacing, memory, and structural awareness.
- Allow separate scene strands to echo, contrast, or eventually connect through repeated language.

**Core Flying Pig Structure**
The Flying Pig version uses three fixed pairs of players.

Each pair owns its own recurring scene strand.

A simple structure:
- Pair A begins the first scene.
- Pair B and Pair C are offstage.
- A player from one offstage pair listens for a line from the active scene.
- One offstage pair repeats a line from the active scene to trigger the other offstage pair.
- The active pair exits.
- The triggered pair enters and begins or resumes its own scene strand.
- Later, another pair repeats a line from the active scene to trigger the remaining pair.
- The form continues, braiding among the three recurring pair-scenes.

**Important Flying Pig Rule**
Pairs do not trigger their own scene.

A pair triggers a different pair’s scene.

For example:
- Pair A is active.
- Pair B and Pair C are offstage.
- Pair C may repeat a line from Pair A.
- That repeated line causes Pair A to exit and Pair B to enter.
- Pair B then begins or resumes its own scene.
- For Pair C to enter later, either Pair A or Pair B would need to repeat a line from the active scene to trigger Pair C.

**How the Form Starts**
- Pair A starts the form.
- Pair B and Pair C do not establish their scene strands first.
- The other pairs appear when triggered.
- When a pair appears for the first time, their repeated trigger line becomes part of the initiation of that pair’s scene.

**Returning Scenes**
- Once a pair’s scene strand has been established, later returns resume that same scene strand.
- The scene may continue from where it left off.
- Time jumps are common.
- The returning pair should justify the repeated line in the context of their own ongoing scene.
- The repeated line is not merely a random initiation; it should become true in the returning scene.

**Example Abstract Pattern**
- Pair A starts Scene A.
- Pair C repeats a line from Scene A, triggering Pair B.
- Pair A exits.
- Pair B enters and starts Scene B, justifying the repeated line.
- Pair A or Pair C repeats a line from Scene B, triggering the remaining pair.
- The active pair exits.
- The triggered pair enters and starts or resumes its own strand.
- The form continues cycling through cross-pair triggers.

**Skills Developed**
- Listening from offstage.
- Scene memory.
- Structural tracking.
- Justification.
- Repetition with transformation.
- Recognizing useful trigger lines.
- Maintaining distinct scene strands.
- Returning to a prior scene with change.
- Time jumps.
- Ensemble coordination.
- Transition clarity.
- Long-form pacing.

**Common Failure Modes**
- Players forget which pair should be triggered.
- A pair accidentally triggers itself.
- Offstage players repeat a line without a clear understanding of which pair should enter.
- The active pair does not recognize that they should exit.
- The triggered pair does not enter quickly.
- Returning scenes do not clearly identify themselves.
- Players treat the repeated line as a brand-new scene every time instead of resuming the same strand.
- Players fail to justify the repeated line in the returning scene.
- Scene strands become too similar.
- Transitions happen before the current scene has established enough information.
- Players return to a scene without advancing it.
- Each return simply repeats the same emotional beat instead of heightening or changing the situation.
- The audience loses track of which scene strand is being resumed.
- The group confuses 3 Rooms with Repeater because both use repeated lines.

**Coaching Notes**
- Each pair needs a distinct scene identity.
- The first appearance of each pair should establish a strong who, what, where, relationship, and emotional tone.
- Offstage players should listen for lines that can be recontextualized.
- The repeated line must be clear enough to function as a transition.
- The triggered pair should enter decisively.
- The exiting pair should leave cleanly.
- Returning to a scene should not mean replaying the same idea.
- Each return should add escalation, consequence, emotional fallout, time jump, status shift, new information, increased pressure, reversal, or payoff.
- Time jumps are useful when they reveal how the repeated line has become true later, earlier, or in a changed circumstance.
- The form works best when the audience can enjoy both:
  - recognition of the repeated line,
  - and surprise at how the new pair justifies it.

**Difference from Montage**
Montage is usually a sequence of independent scenes inspired by an opening or suggestion. Scenes may call back, but they do not have to return.

3 Rooms is more structured. Scene strands are expected to interrupt, return, and interweave.

**Difference from La Ronde**
La Ronde is a character chain: one character remains while a new player enters, creating a sequence of two-person scenes.

3 Rooms is not primarily about one character staying. It is about multiple fixed pair scene strands being interrupted and resumed.

**Difference from Repeater**
Repeater uses a repeated line from inside the active scene as the edit into a new scene. The player who repeats the line transforms into a new character/context and causes their current scene partner to exit.

3 Rooms uses repeated lines as cross-pair triggers. The pair repeating the line does not trigger its own scene; it triggers another pair to enter or return.

The Flying Pig catalog entry here refers specifically to the long-form/offstage-line-repeat structure, not the short-form switching game.

### Slacker
**Type:** Long Form Structure  
**Standard:** Yes, with Flying Pig tag-out character-chain version documented here  
**Common Names:** Slacker; Tag-Out Slacker; Character Chain  
**Observed In:** Flying Pig Classes / Practices / Long Form Sets  
**Observed Frequency:** Witnessed often

**Purpose**
- Build a long-form set through connected two-person scenes.
- Practice preserving one character while revealing them through new relationships.
- Train players to create immediate new relationship offers.
- Develop character continuity without requiring a single plot.
- Practice tag-out transitions.
- Use time jumps to reveal different sides of a character.
- Build momentum through a chain of relationships.
- Develop ensemble awareness, editing judgment, and structural flow.

**Core Flying Pig Structure**
Flying Pig’s version follows the character who stays.

A two-person scene begins. An offstage player physically tags out one of the two players. The tagged player exits. The entering player starts a new scene with the character who stayed.

The new scene creates a new relationship with the character who remained onstage.

Then another offstage player may tag out one of those players, leaving the other character to continue into the next scene.

**Example Structure**
- Scene 1: Dentist and patient argue.
- An offstage player taps out the dentist.
- The patient stays.
- The entering player becomes a wedding planner and tells the patient to get his cameras ready.
- The patient is now revealed as a photographer.
- Then another player taps out the patient/photographer.
- The wedding planner stays.
- The entering player becomes the bride and argues with the wedding planner.
- Then another player taps out the wedding planner.
- The bride stays.
- The entering player becomes the bride’s sister and talks about hooking up with a groomsman after the wedding.

**Rules / Flying Pig Structure**
- Two players begin a scene.
- An offstage player physically tags or taps out one of the players.
- The tapped player exits.
- The entering player starts a new scene with the player who stayed.
- The new scene should establish a new relationship with the character who stayed.
- The entering player does not need to be someone mentioned or implied in the previous scene.
- The entering player may invent a new relationship for the staying character.
- The new relationship is usually established right away.
- Time jumps are very common.
- The chain continues as players tag out one character and create a new relationship with the character who remains.

**Important Flying Pig Rule**
- The form follows the character who stayed, not the character who left.
- This corrects the earlier catalog description that described Slacker as following a character, object, idea, sound, location, or thread out of the prior scene.
- In the Flying Pig version documented here, the key mechanism is character continuity through tag-outs.

**Time Jumps**
Time jumps are common and useful.

A new scene may happen:
- later that day,
- years later,
- before the previous scene,
- immediately after an unseen event,
- during a major life transition,
- after a relationship has changed,
- or in any moment that reveals a new side of the staying character.

The time jump should help clarify a new relationship, consequence, status shift, or emotional change.

**Entering Player Responsibilities**
- Physically tag out one of the active players.
- Enter with a clear new relationship to the character who stayed.
- Establish the relationship quickly, usually right away.
- Give the staying character a new context.
- Avoid vague initiations.
- Avoid making the new scene feel like the same relationship with a different face.
- Use the staying character’s prior behavior as fuel, not as a cage.

**Staying Player Responsibilities**
- Preserve the same character.
- Let the new relationship reveal a different side of that character.
- Accept the new context immediately.
- Do not reset into a generic character.
- Carry forward useful emotional, status, or behavioral information from prior scenes when appropriate.
- Allow time jumps to affect the character without making them unrecognizable.

**Skills Developed**
- Character continuity.
- Relationship initiation.
- Tag-out editing.
- Listening.
- Time jumps.
- Status and relationship variety.
- Ensemble awareness.
- Character memory.
- Fast who/what/where.
- Revealing different facets of a character.
- Letting scenes connect through people rather than plot.

**Common Failure Modes**
- The staying player changes character instead of preserving the same one.
- The entering player does not establish a clear new relationship.
- The new scene feels like a continuation of the same relationship rather than a new context.
- Players tag out the wrong person for the scene they want to create.
- Players hesitate after the tag instead of initiating clearly.
- Time jumps are unclear.
- The form becomes loose montage because character continuity is not preserved.
- The entering player waits for the staying character to define the relationship.
- The staying character ignores the gift of the new relationship.
- The chain becomes plot-heavy instead of relationship-driven.
- Players misunderstand the form as following mentioned characters rather than following the character who stays.

**Coaching Notes**
- The audience should understand who stayed and what new relationship they are now in.
- The new relationship should be established quickly.
- The form works best when each new scene reveals a different side of the staying character.
- Strong initiations often name or imply relationship immediately:
  - “Dad…”
  - “Officer…”
  - “Bridezilla, your guests are revolting.”
  - “Professor, the committee rejected your theory.”
  - “Sister, you promised you would not tell Mom.”
- Time jumps should be used to create development, not confusion.
- The entering player should give the staying character something playable right away.
- The staying player should preserve the character while letting the new scene affect them.
- The best Slacker chains feel like the audience is discovering a network of relationships around one character at a time.
- The form should evolve through relationships, consequences, status shifts, and emotional reveals rather than mechanical tagging alone.

**Difference from Montage**
Montage jumps by inspiration.

Flying Pig Slacker follows a staying character through a new relationship created by a tag-out.

**Difference from La Ronde**
La Ronde is also a character-chain structure in which one character continues into the next scene.

Flying Pig Slacker, as documented here, overlaps strongly with that family, but its remembered classroom identity is “Slacker,” and its practical teaching focus is the physical offstage tag-out into a new relationship with the character who stayed.

**Difference from Character-Mention Chain**
The previously documented “Character-Mention Chain” was not a separate Flying Pig game. It was an earlier misunderstanding of Slacker.

For catalog purposes, Character-Mention Chain should not remain as an independent entry. The important correction is that Slacker does not require the new player to enter as someone mentioned in the previous scene. The new player creates a new relationship with the character who stayed.

**Flying Pig Specific Notes**
- Flying Pig’s version follows the character who stayed.
- An offstage player physically tags out one active player.
- The entering player creates a new relationship with the character who remains.
- The new relationship is usually established right away.
- The entering player does not have to be a mentioned or implied character from the prior scene.
- Time jumps are very common.
- Troy initially confused this with a separate Character-Mention Chain form, but later clarified that they are the same game and that the operating name is Slacker.
- The central teaching point should be: tag one character out, keep one character onstage, and reveal the staying character through a new relationship.
- This form fits Troy’s structural strengths because it rewards continuity tracking, clean transitions, and relationship framing.
- Troy’s growth edge is making each new relationship emotionally or relationally playable, not just mechanically connected.

**What Success Looks Like**
- Each tag-out is clear.
- The audience knows which character stayed.
- The entering player establishes a new relationship quickly.
- The staying character remains recognizable.
- Time jumps clarify rather than confuse.
- Each new relationship reveals something new.
- The chain feels connected through character, not forced plot.

### Two Chairs
**Type:** Long Form Structure / Object-Transformation Structure  
**Standard:** Unknown / Flying Pig observed long-form structure  
**Observed In:** Long Form Practice / Long Form Sets  
**Player Count:** 6 players  
**Status:** Actual Flying Pig name

**Purpose**
- Practice building scenes from physical stage pictures.
- Train players to justify unusual object configurations immediately.
- Develop bold environment work with minimal set pieces.
- Encourage players to treat ordinary chairs as flexible scenic objects.
- Build physical interaction, object work, and grounded emotional reality inside surreal or unexpected premises.
- Create a sequence of distinct scenes linked by chair rearrangement rather than plot continuity.

**Setup**
- Two chairs are placed on stage in an unusual configuration.
- The configuration may include chairs being:
  - upside down,
  - tilted,
  - sideways,
  - stacked or partially stacked,
  - facing odd directions,
  - separated at strange distances,
  - or otherwise arranged in a way that suggests an object, location, obstacle, creature, vehicle, machine, or piece of environment.
- Six players participate.
- Two players are active in each scene.
- The remaining four players watch from offstage and may call edits.

**Rules / Sequence of Play**
1. Two players enter and initiate a scene.
2. The players treat the chair configuration as an object, environment, or physical reality inspired by the way the chairs are positioned.
3. The chairs may become almost anything, such as:
   - a vehicle,
   - a creature,
   - a strange piece of furniture,
   - a machine,
   - a natural formation,
   - an obstacle,
   - a shrine,
   - a cockpit,
   - a trap,
   - or another scenic object.
4. The players justify and interact with the chairs in context.
5. Any player not currently in the scene may call the edit.
6. When the edit is called, the two active players reset the chairs into a new unusual configuration.
7. After resetting the chairs, the active players exit.
8. Two new players enter.
9. The new players interpret the new chair configuration as an entirely different object, situation, or world.
10. The new scene should be unrelated to the prior scene.
11. The process repeats, with each new chair arrangement inspiring a fresh scene.

**Important Structural Rule**
- The chairs are the transition device.
- The outgoing players do not simply leave after the edit.
- Before exiting, they create the next visual offer by rearranging the chairs.
- The incoming players then accept that configuration as the foundation of a new, unrelated scene.

**Skills Developed**
- Object work.
- Space work.
- Physical justification.
- Scene initiation from visual information.
- Environmental commitment.
- Physical play.
- Edit recognition.
- Ensemble support.
- Creating distinct worlds quickly.
- Grounding surreal stage pictures in playable reality.
- Treating set pieces as offers rather than props.

**Common Failure Modes**
- Players ignore the chair configuration and begin a generic scene.
- Players identify the object but do not physically interact with it.
- Players make the chairs into something unclear to the audience.
- Players over-explain what the chairs are instead of using them.
- Scenes become disconnected from the physical offer after the first few lines.
- Players make each new scene too similar to the previous one.
- Outgoing players reset the chairs into a configuration that is too neutral or visually uninteresting.
- Incoming players hesitate because they are trying to find the perfect interpretation.
- Players focus on clever object identification but do not build relationship, stakes, or emotional reality.
- The edit is called before the scene has enough time to establish the chair reality.
- The edit is called too late after the scene has already stalled.

**Coaching Tips**
- Justify the chairs immediately.
- Whatever the configuration suggests, accept it as the scene’s reality.
- Bold choices are better than cautious interpretations.
- Players should physically interact with the chairs, not merely talk about them.
- The object should affect behavior, relationship, status, danger, desire, or emotional stakes.
- Each new scene should be distinct from the one before it.
- The outgoing players should give the next pair a strong visual gift through the chair reset.
- The incoming players should not wait for certainty; they should make a clear choice and commit.
- Even if the object is surreal, the scene should still have emotional grounding.
- Success depends on the audience being able to understand the logic of the new reality quickly.

**What Success Looks Like**
- Each chair arrangement inspires a distinct scene.
- The audience can clearly understand what the chairs have become.
- Players physically use the chairs in a committed, readable way.
- The new realities are surprising but coherent.
- Scenes feel emotionally grounded even when the object interpretation is strange.
- Edits feel clean because the outgoing players create a strong visual offer for the next scene.
- The form has variety, momentum, and strong visual play.

**Flying Pig Specific Notes**
- The actual Flying Pig name is Two Chairs.
- Flying Pig runs this as a six-player long-form structure.
- Two players are active in each scene.
- Any player not currently in the scene may call the edit.
- The players currently in the scene reset the chairs before exiting.
- The next pair enters and treats the new chair arrangement as an entirely new object, situation, or world.
- New scenes are unrelated to prior scenes.
- The form rewards players who can read physical offers quickly, commit to environmental reality, and build emotional stakes from object work.

### Scramble
**Type:** Longform Format / Longform Exercise  
**Standard:** Known longform improv format; Flying Pig/show usage observed once  
**Source Basis:** Improv Resource Center / IRC Improv Wiki rules, plus Troy observation  
**Observed In:** Show performance  
**Observed Player Count:** 6 players  
**Status:** Partially documented; standard rules imported, observed local variant still needs confirmation

**Purpose**
- Create a chaotic longform structure using overlapping scenes.
- Train ensemble awareness under disorder.
- Practice maintaining commitment while other scenes happen simultaneously.
- Build stage pictures with multiple active realities.
- Encourage players to embrace confusion without losing their own scene.
- Allow the audience to interpret and absorb overlapping material.

**Standard Scramble Description**
- Scramble is a longform improvisational format built around overlapping scenes and onstage chaos.
- The form is intentionally confusing and disorderly.
- Improvisers are encouraged to stir up disorder and accept that they cannot understand everything happening on stage.
- The audience is left to interpret and absorb the overlapping action.
- The show may or may not begin with an opening.
- It typically begins with a solo start.
- From there, players use techniques that create a world of overlapping scenes.

**Observed / Remembered Flying Pig or Show Details**
- Troy saw this performed in a show roughly eight months before documenting it.
- The performers called it "Scramble."
- Troy is confident it was done with 6 players.
- Exact local rules, opening, editing style, and show-specific constraints are not fully remembered.

**Core Techniques**

*1. Solo Starts*
- One player begins alone on stage.
- The player makes a large physical and emotional choice.
- The player interacts with an invisible character who is understood to be in the scene.
- The player leaves space for the invisible character's lines to land.
- The player reacts as if the invisible character is responding.

*2. Overlap Edits*
- A player initiates a new solo start on top of an existing scene or scenes.
- The previous scene does not necessarily end.
- The new initiation overlaps with what is already happening.
- These overlap edits lead to stacked scenes.

*3. Stacked Scenes*
- Multiple scenes happen at the same time on stage.
- The scenes may occupy or use the same physical space.
- Players may playfully violate the location or object-work reality of another scene.
- Each stacked scene benefits from having a distinct vibe, energy, tone, or rhythm so the audience can perceive contrast.

*4. Bolts*
- A player definitively leaves a scene by breaking eye contact and exiting with intent.
- The performer leaves, but the character they were playing is still understood to remain in the scene.
- This leaves the scene in the hands of the remaining performer or performers.
- The remaining player may continue interacting with the now-invisible character.

*5. Swinging Gates*
- A player uses bolts to move between scenes.
- The player may swing from one scene into another and eventually switch back.
- The player should fully commit to the scene they are currently in rather than trying to half-play multiple scenes at once.
- The player trusts that reacting to the next offer in the current scene will guide them.

*6. Chair Pimps*
- A player places one or more chairs somewhere on stage.
- The chair placement implies that a future scene will incorporate the chair.
- The chair acts as a planted scenic offer for later use.

**Rules / Performance Principles**
- Embrace confusion.
- Do not try to understand everything happening on stage.
- Commit fully to the scene you are currently in.
- Make bold physical and emotional choices.
- Let overlapping realities coexist.
- Use contrast so stacked scenes do not blur together.
- Maintain enough clarity that the audience has something to follow.
- Treat chaos as structured ensemble play, not permission to ignore others.

**Skills Developed**
- Longform structure.
- Ensemble awareness.
- Solo initiation.
- Emotional and physical commitment.
- Working with invisible characters.
- Scene overlap.
- Stage picture.
- Editing through initiation.
- Comfort with ambiguity.
- Multi-thread listening.
- Character continuity.
- Audience focus management.

**Common Failure Modes**
- Players create noise without playable structure.
- Multiple scenes overlap but have the same energy, making them hard to distinguish.
- Players abandon emotional commitment because the form feels chaotic.
- Players try to track everything instead of committing to their immediate scene.
- Bolts become vague exits instead of clear intentional departures.
- Invisible characters are not treated as real.
- Chair pimps are planted but never used.
- The audience cannot tell where to focus.
- Players use chaos as an excuse to ignore relationship, stakes, or physical clarity.

**Coaching Notes**
- The form needs strong commitment, not casual randomness.
- Solo starts should be emotionally and physically bold.
- Invisible characters must be treated as real scene partners.
- In stacked scenes, contrast matters: different tempo, volume, posture, emotion, or tone helps the audience separate the threads.
- Players should not apologize for confusion; confusion is part of the form.
- However, the audience still needs readable behavior, clear commitment, and strong stage pictures.
- Bolting should be decisive enough that the audience understands the performer left while the character may still remain in the scene.
- Chair placements should be specific enough that later players can use them.

**What Success Looks Like**
- The stage feels alive and chaotic but not dead random.
- Multiple scene realities coexist.
- Players stay emotionally committed.
- The audience can absorb patterns, contrasts, and fragments.
- Solo starts and overlap edits generate momentum.
- Bolts and swinging gates create fluid movement between scenes.
- Chair pimps pay off later.
- The form feels like a controlled scramble rather than a pileup.

**Source Notes**
- Standard rule structure imported from the Improv Resource Center / IRC Improv Wiki entry on "Scramble."
- The IRC page describes Scramble as a longform format featuring overlapping scenes and chaos.
- The IRC page lists at least six techniques: Solo Starts, Overlap Edits, Stacked Scenes, Bolts, Swinging Gates, and Chair Pimps.
- Player count of 6 is from Troy's memory of the observed show, not from the IRC source.

### Conducted Song / Show-Closing Conducted Song
**Type:** Show Closing / Musical-Rhythmic Short Form Closer  
**Standard:** Common short-form family with Flying Pig show-closing variation  
**Common Names:** Conducted Song; Show-Closing Conducted Song  
**Observed In:** Show Closings  
**Observed Frequency:** Only observed as a show closer

**Purpose**
- End a show by recycling memorable lines from earlier scenes.
- Create a musical or rhythmic callback to the whole performance.
- Let the audience hear silly show lines out of context.
- Build a final ensemble moment from shared rhythm, repetition, and callbacks.
- Give the show closer a theatrical way to button the performance.
- Create a sense that the show’s scattered moments have become one final group piece.

**Setup**
- One player serves as the conductor.
- The conductor is usually the person ending the show.
- The conductor was also a performer in the show earlier; they are not an outside musical director.
- The other players stand in a line facing the audience.
- The conductor stands in front of the line and faces the players.

**Sequence of Play**
1. The conductor stands in front of the ensemble.
2. The conductor points to one player.
3. That player chooses a line from earlier in the show.
4. The chosen line is usually a line that was especially silly, memorable, or funny when taken out of context.
5. The player sings or rhythmically repeats the line.
6. The conductor points to a second player.
7. The second player chooses a different remembered line from the show and begins repeating it.
8. The conductor continues assigning players by pointing until each player has claimed a line.
9. Players repeat their lines within a shared improvised rhythm or tune.
10. Stronger singers may add melody or harmony.
11. Less musical players may participate more rhythmically than melodically.
12. Once all players have their lines, the conductor conducts the group by cueing players in and out and adjusting the performance.
13. The conductor may signal louder, softer, slower, faster, or other changes.
14. The conductor may cue players to change their lines.
15. When changing a line, a player may keep part of their original line while borrowing part from another player’s line.
16. The conductor ends the piece by cutting everyone off together.

**Line-Claiming Phase**
- Players choose their own lines.
- The conductor does not assign the specific line.
- Earlier players in the order have first access to the most obvious favorite lines.
- Players later in the order may lose the line they wanted because another player already claimed it.
- Players have been known to gripe after shows about being later in the order because someone else took the line they wanted.

**Conductor Role**
- Select players by pointing.
- Establish the order in which lines are claimed.
- Listen to the emerging rhythm.
- Bring players in and out.
- Shape volume, tempo, and energy.
- Cue louder and softer sections.
- Cue faster and slower sections.
- Encourage line changes or line blending when useful.
- Build toward a final ensemble sound.
- End decisively by cutting everyone off together.

**Conductor Gesture Style**
- The conductor uses the best gestures they can.
- The gestures are functional theatrical signals rather than fixed or formal conducting technique.
- The conductor may use pointing, hand height, arm size, tempo gestures, facial expression, body energy, and other intuitive signals.
- The group responds to the apparent intention rather than to formal music-conducting vocabulary.

**Singer / Player Responsibilities**
- Pick a memorable line from earlier in the show.
- Repeat the line rhythmically or musically.
- Stay aware of the shared rhythm.
- Respond to the conductor’s cues.
- Keep repeating until cued otherwise.
- Adjust volume, tempo, or delivery as signaled.
- When cued to change the line, preserve part of the original while borrowing or blending with another player’s line.
- Support the group sound rather than trying to dominate it.

**Musical / Rhythmic Style**
- The group uses a shared improvised tune or rhythm.
- The piece can work even when not all players are strong singers.
- It is often more rhythmic than formally musical.
- Strong singers have room to add melody, harmony, or musical shape.
- The comedy often comes from repetition and absurd juxtaposition rather than polished singing.

**Skills Developed**
- Listening.
- Callback recognition.
- Ensemble rhythm.
- Musical or rhythmic support.
- Following a conductor.
- Volume and tempo control.
- Group awareness.
- Show memory.
- Line selection.
- Ending cleanly.

**Common Failure Modes**
- Players choose lines that are not memorable enough.
- Players choose lines that are too long to repeat cleanly.
- Multiple players want the same obvious line.
- Players fail to lock into a shared rhythm.
- Non-singers become self-conscious and reduce commitment.
- Strong singers overpower the group instead of supporting it.
- Conductor gestures are unclear.
- Players miss cues to enter, stop, soften, grow, speed up, slow down, or change lines.
- The piece drags if the conductor does not shape it.
- The ending becomes mushy if the conductor does not cut everyone off clearly.

**Coaching Notes**
- Choose short, repeatable lines.
- The line does not have to be witty by itself; silliness out of context is often enough.
- Repetition creates the game.
- The conductor should be bold and clear, even without formal conducting skills.
- Players should watch the conductor closely.
- The piece works best when everyone treats the conducted changes as real offers.
- Musical polish is less important than rhythm, confidence, and ensemble agreement.
- The cutoff ending should be decisive.

**Flying Pig Specific Notes**
- Flying Pig uses this only as a show closer in the documented experience.
- The conductor is usually the person who ends the show.
- The conductor was also one of the players in the show earlier.
- Players stand in a line while the conductor stands in front of them.
- Each player chooses their own line from earlier in the show.
- Lines are usually especially silly or funny out of context.
- Earlier players in the line-claiming order may take the most desirable lines.
- The group creates a shared improvised rhythm or tune.
- Some players harmonize or add melody when able.
- The conductor uses improvised theatrical gestures rather than fixed conducting signals.
- The conductor may cue louder, softer, slower, faster, player selection, and line changes.
- Players may change lines by keeping part of their line and borrowing part from another player’s line.
- The conductor ends by cutting everyone off together.

**What Success Looks Like**
- The audience recognizes lines from earlier in the show.
- The repeated lines become funny or satisfying in combination.
- The group finds a shared rhythm.
- The conductor shapes the piece clearly.
- Musical players add texture without making non-singers look bad.
- The closer feels like a final callback to the whole show.
- The group ends together on a clean cutoff.

## General Flying Pig Lessons

1. Make eye contact with your scene partner.
2. Speak loudly so you’re heard.
3. Yes, and—always accept and build.
4. Initiate scenes with who, what, when, and where.
5. React naturally first — don’t invent reactions. Use yourself as the base, then apply a filter such as accent, age, status, etc.
6. Take audience gifts from A to C—expand on them.
7. Start scenes so they could run 40 minutes—anchor the world.
8. Find the game—find what’s funny or unusual, then repeat and heighten it.
9. Rule of three—if a reaction happens twice, do it a third time.
10. Sell it—fully commit.
11. Characters are as smart as you are—no dumbing down.
12. Introduce characters with a body part.
13. Big gifting—be specific in actions or character definitions.
14. Pimping—bring others in with clear roles.
15. When asking for gifts, always say, “May I please have…”
16. Shoot the Deer — don’t talk around doing something; do it.
17. Re-anchor in character — don’t spend too much time talking about the past or things that aren’t happening in the scene. If that starts to happen, re-anchor in the relationship.
18. Take a physical action — if you don’t know what to say, immediately take a physical action. This is the default recovery tool.

## Curriculum Themes Observed So Far

### 1. Clear structure supports risk
Flying Pig often uses highly structured games to create a safe container for risk: spelling patterns, alphabet order, chair inheritance, audience lines, producer intervention, and repeat-line edits.

### 2. Physical commitment is treated as central
Many exercises emphasize visible body use: Pass the Face, Space and Object Work, 3 Chairs, Sit/Stand/Lay, Bunny Bunny, and the meditation’s body awareness.

### 3. Fast recovery matters more than perfection
Games like Oh Waiter, Home Shopping Network, Alphabet Game, Mind Meld, and Bunny Bunny reward moving quickly after mistakes.

### 4. Audience care is a repeated hidden skill
Pillars, HSN, Lines from a Box, and show-opening language all require players to make audience participation feel safe and successful.

### 5. Jesse’s teaching pushes large readability
Repeated notes suggest Jesse favors physically and emotionally readable choices over subtle realism. This is especially visible in 3 Chairs, the opening meditation, and emotion-heavy class notes.

### 6. Advanced work often fails at transitions before it fails at scene content
Repeater, Montage, Breaking News as a long-form insert, and Town Hall all depend on clean transitions, edits, and timing judgment.

### 7. Hosting and producer roles are real performance skills
HSN, Spelling Bee, Duets, Master Debater, Town Hall, Foreign Film, Slideshow, and New Choice all depend heavily on a player who can frame, pace, rescue, call, and end the game cleanly.

### 8. Constraint games reveal specific technical growth
Alphabet Game, Number of Words, Gibberish Switch, Entrances and Exits, New Choice, and Growing and Shrinking all test whether players can preserve scene work while managing a visible technical constraint. These games often expose whether players can keep relationship, stakes, and emotional readability alive while tracking rules.

### 9. Scene-memory forms require clearer teaching progressions
3 Rooms (French Braid), Slacker, La Ronde, Repeater, and Growing and Shrinking all depend on players remembering the active structure. These forms can fail at the rules-transmission layer before they fail at creativity or scene work. Diagrams, simple first reps, and explicit transition rules would improve practice quality.
